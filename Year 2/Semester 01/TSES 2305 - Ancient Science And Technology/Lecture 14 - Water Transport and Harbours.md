### KEY DISTINCTIONS & TERMINOLOGY

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Boat**|Small vessel that can be put on a ship; river/lake traffic|
|**Ship**|Large vessel; Mediterranean/Atlantic sailing|
|**Tacking**|Sailing crab-wise at an angle (up to 60°) against the wind|
|**Mortise & tenon**|Joinery technique; dowels and tenons swell in mortise → waterproof|
|**Skeleton construction**|Keel first, then ribs, then planking; post-AD 719|
|**Shell construction**|Hull first, then ribs; pre-AD 719; has **carling** instead of keel|

---

### 2. CONSTRUCTION TECHNIQUES

#### Shell Construction (older method)

- Built from outside in.
    
- No central keel; has **carling** (longitudinal beam).
    
- Mortise and tenon + ropes + nails.
    
- Hull has elasticity → can be beached.
    
- Examples: **Yenikapi YK12, YK27, YK11** (AD vii–ix).
    

#### Skeleton Construction (newer method, after AD 719)

- Built from inside out: keel → ribs → planking.
    
- Nailed, not mortise/tenon; requires pitch for waterproofing.
    
- Faster, less skilled labour, standardizable.
    
- Cannot be beached (rounded bottom); must dock.
    
- Examples: **Yenikapi YK14**, Fiumicino wrecks.
    

#### Mortise & Tenon Details

- Same technique as fine furniture.
    
- Swelling of wood makes join waterproof without pitch.
    
- Repairs often done with nails (visible in irregular patterns).

### 3. BOATS (River & Lake)

#### Log Boats (Fens, England, c. 1500 BC)

- Oldest surviving boats in Europe.
    
- Dugout canoes; shallow, calm waters.
    

#### Egyptian Nile Craft

- Reed boats (high prow/stern because reeds).
    
- Sailed downstream, rowed/towed upstream.
    
- **Mark Twain** = “twain man” calling depth.
    
- Tomb of **Mekerte** (1981 BC): magical reconstitution figures → accurate details.
    

#### Roman River Craft

|Type|Features|
|---|---|
|_Caudicariae_ (Ostia, AD ii)|Pulled on tow path; high prow/stern; had own guild|
|Mainz patrol boat (AD iii/iv)|Outrigger to protect oars; no sails; beached|
|Arles boat (AD iii)|Flat bottom; punting planks; single sail; tiller at centre|
|Mainz trade boat (AD iii)|No mast; towed upstream; small cargo hold|

#### Tiber Island (Rome)

- Furthest point navigable inland.
    
- Rome’s oldest road = _via salaria_ (salt road).
    
- Cargo offloaded into _Horrea Galbae_ or _Porticus Aemilia_.

### 4. SHIPS (Sea-Going)

#### Bronze Age (Minoan/Mycenaean)

- **Thera Admiral’s Flotilla Fresco** (c. 1700 BC): boats look like Nile craft but with dolphins = sea context.
    
- **Chania replica Minoan ship**: commissioned Greek navy; carried 2004 Olympic torch.
    
- **Kommos anchors** (Syrian type, c. 1400–1300 BC): square hole for ropes; wooden flukes.
    

#### Geometric & Archaic Greece

|Artifact|Date|Features|
|---|---|---|
|Mokhlos clay model|c. 3000/2700 BC|Ram at water line = warship|
|Skyros “frying pan”|c. 2500–2000 BC|Ship with high prow + many oars|
|Athens aryballos #14177|c. 690 BC|Upside-down painting; joy at return to harbour|
|Ischia boat inv. 238621|600s BC|Corinthian clay; possible warship|

#### Classical Greek Warships

- **Trireme** (3 banks of oars): crucial for Athenian grain empire.
    
- **Lenormant Relief** (c. 410 BC, Acropolis Museum): shows trireme with three banks; found near Erechtheion.
    
- **Arsenal of Philo** (Piraeus, 346–330 BC): held 196 triremes; large stele with specifications.
    
- **Olympias** (1987 replica): 36.9 m long; 9 knots top speed; 180° turn in 1 minute.
    

#### Hellenistic Arms Race

- City-states and kingdoms built ever-larger ships.
    
- Fighting towers + catapults required catamarans (two pentekonters tied together).
    

#### Roman Warships

- Romans never thought of themselves as sailors; navy mostly Greek.
    
- Strategy: turn sea battle into land battle (_corvus_, _harpax_).
    
- By Empire: patrol boats (biremes, pentekonters) against pirates.
    
- **Thamaguda mosaic** (Tunisia, AD iii): pentekonter with lateen sail and bored eye on prow.
    
- **House of the Vettii frescoes**: Roman patrol boats with tragic masks (female, male, snoring Silenus).

### 5. COMMERCIAL SHIPS (Merchantmen)

#### Grain Ships

- **Ostia fresco _Isis Giminiana_** (c. AD 250): grain ship; captain (_magister_) Farnaces; owner Arascanius.
    
- Lucian’s _The Ship_: comic inflation – 55 m long, 12,000 metric tonnes (4× reasonable).
    
- **Coin of Commodus** (AD 180–192): Pharos lighthouse + grain ship.
    
- **Lod mosaic** (Israel, AD iii): two grain ships sailing opposite directions; fantasy sea creatures.
    

#### Cargo & Wild Animals

- **Ostia funeral relief** (AD ii): _corbita_ merchantman furling sails; lions in cages for amphitheatre games.
    
- Cicero on tigers: “hunted out of existence” in Turkey for Roman games.
    
- Livy: lions jumped barrier in Forum, killed three senators.
    

#### Anchor Technology

- Wood stocks (iv BC) → lead stocks dominate (ii BC – ii AD).
    
- Lead arms often decorated with names, letters, deities.
    
- **Ischia Museum**: anchor stocks + corpse weight (Gulf of Pozzuoli).

### 6. HARBOURS & PORT FACILITIES

#### Lasaia (Crete)

- Traphos island joined to mainland by **causeway** → double-sided harbour.
    
- 27 warehouses = major shipping port.
    
- Concrete front wall (Roman reinforcement).
    
- Marble revetment = “marble means money.”
    
- Sherds: Italian Terra Sigillata, African Red Slip → Roman merchants present.
    

#### Piraeus (Athens)

- **Zea Harbour**: military harbour; ship sheds for triremes.
    
- **Mounichia ship slip**: preserved.
    
- **Rethymnon (Crete) ship slip** (Mike Curtis excavation).
    

#### Other Harbours

- **Souda (Chania, Crete)**: natural harbour; sunken yacht _Coral of Cowes_ (1902, sunk 2021) – suspicious death.
    
- **Venice arsenali**: comparison for Bronze Age ship sheds (thin, narrow buildings).

### SAILING SEASON & NAVIGATION

#### Sailing Windows (Hesiod, _Works and Days_)

|Period|Dates|Conditions|
|---|---|---|
|Early spring|21 Mar – 20 Jun|Unpredictable storms|
|Optimal|21 Jun – 31 Aug|Best sailing|
|Meltemi|from 1 Sep|Strong head winds|
|Pleiades|from 15 Oct|Beginning of rain|

#### Tacking

- Cannot sail directly into wind.
    
- Can sail up to 60° against wind.
    
- “Three sheets to the wind” = three sails best for tacking.
    
- **Ostia reliefs**: sailors furling sails in storm; _corbita_ with main and sprit sail.
    

#### Losses at Sea

- Estimated **50%** of ships lost.
    
- Zeno the Stoic lost entire cargo.
    
- Lloyd’s registry (1820–1880) confirms high ancient estimates.

### 9. CASE STUDY: HERCULANEUM (AD 79)

- **Ship sheds** preserved by Vesuvius; 238 corpses found inside.
    
- Fishing boat (Tyrrhenian Sea): 2 m+ long; 3 oarlocks; mortise and tenon; copper nails capped by pegs.
    
- Woods: pine (inner), alder (outer), beech (frame), oak (planks).
    
- **Amphorae from ship sheds**: record of imports/exports and reuse.
    
- **Rope & cable** from bath: triple strands; pine tar/pitch waterproofing.
    

---

### 10. CASE STUDY: YENIKAPI (ISTANBUL)

- Excavations for subway tunnel; now museum at entry.
    
- **YK12 (AD vii–x)**: merchantman, shell construction, cargo intact.
    
- **YK27 (AD vii–x)**: shell construction; flat bottom; carling visible.
    
- **YK11 (AD ix)**: sometimes misreported as YK14; double skin; shell.
    
- **YK14 (AD ix)**: skeleton construction; keel + carling; wooden notebooks and games on board.
    

**Transition point**: after AD 719, shell → skeleton construction.

---

### 11. CASE STUDY: LAKE NEMI PLEASURE BARGES (CALIGULA, AD 39/40)

- Two floating palaces: marble, mosaic, heating, plumbing, baths.
    
- Advanced tech: nails, brass rails, lions’ head oar locks.
    
- Too large to turn; likely never used (scuttled after Caligula’s assassination).
    
- Destroyed by retreating German army (31 May 1944).
