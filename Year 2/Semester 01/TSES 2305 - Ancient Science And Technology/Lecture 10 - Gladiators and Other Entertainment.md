## ART 1: GLADIATORIAL DEITIES

Gladiatorial deities were largely **military deities** — the arena was understood as a sacred space where combat honored the gods.

### Orion (Syncretism with Mithras)

- Created by Apollo, Ares, and Hermes "in a fit of boredom" as a lesser god destined to cause trouble
- In Greek myth, Orion is known almost exclusively as a rapist and would-be rapist of the **Pleiades** (the 7 daughters of Pleias) — they chose to be turned into stars rather than submit to him; as a constellation, Orion perpetually faces the Pleiades
- His reputation as a master hunter and warrior was largely absorbed by **Mithras**
- Mithras is an import from India (mentioned in the Vedic scriptures); became the **chief deity of the Roman army**
- As a trinitarian deity (with Indra and Ahura Mazda), the Mithras cult — alongside Isis-Serapis-Osiris — was the main religious challenger to early Christianity

### Key Gladiatorial Patron Deities

- **Mithras** — primary deity of soldiers and gladiators; Walbrook Mithraeum (London, in the Roman camp) now located on the ground floor of the Bloomsbury publishing house
- **Hephaistos (Vulcan)** — despite being lame, venerated as smith of the gods and main divine armourer; bowl fragment from Royal Ontario Museum (ROM 929.49.33, ca. AD 110–120) shows Hephaistos with a gladiator
- **Hercules** — patron deity of gladiators; his connection with combat in the Roman world is primarily as a wrestler and fighter; tombstone from Ostia (AD I/II) dedicated to Hercules as patron
- **Nemesis** — goddess of fate and retribution, closely associated with the _vanquished_ in combat
    - **Eastern style** (Gortyn, Crete, Grand Roman Theatre): shown with cobra and griffin; statues/reliefs placed near the _aditus maximus_ (loser's gate), away from the presiding magistrate's tribunal; also equated with **Livia**, wife of Augustus, in the Imperial Cult
    - **Western style** (Louvre): foot always on a wheel; watches a gladiator die

## PART 2: LAWS GOVERNING GLADIATORS

### Spartacus and the Servile Wars

- The **Third Servile War (73–71 BC)** was an uprising of gladiators led by the Thracian Spartacus
- The pirate fleet sent to help Spartacus **sacked Ostia in 69 BC**
- In response, laws were passed forbidding gladiators from being housed **inside city walls**

> **Film note:** Kirk Douglas (_Spartacus_, 1960) and Andy Whitfield (_Spartacus_, 2010) are the most famous portrayals.

### Senatorial Decree on Gladiatorial Costs (AD 177)

**SC de pretiis gladiatorum minuendis** (CIL II 6278 = ILS 5163) — issued near the end of Marcus Aurelius's reign, inscribed on two bronze tablets from Italica (Spain); one tablet lost in the Spanish Civil War; 63 lines survive.

**Key provisions:**

- Shifted much of the cost of gladiatorial games from local officials to the emperor
- **Forbade combat to the death** without special permission from the emperor, case by case
- Established a **tiered pay scale** based on game budget:

|Game Budget (HS)|Top Pay|Mid Pay|Low Pay|
|---|---|---|---|
|Up to 30,000|Unchanged|—|—|
|30,000–60,000|5,000|4,000|3,000|
|60,000–100,000|8,000|6,000|5,000|
|100,000–150,000|12,000|10,000|8,000/6,000/5,000|
|Over 150,000|15,000|12,000|9,000/7,000/6,000|

_(HS = sestertii, the standard Roman coin)_

**Broader effects on civic life:**

- Cost of games previously fell to **aediles** (mid-tier elected officials)
- Emperors increasingly restricted gladiatorial combat to events celebrating the **imperial cult**, making it harder for rivals to use games to gain political popularity
- Costs of the imperial cult were borne privately by **wealthy freed slaves (imperial freedmen)** — some faced bankruptcy from the expense
- The decree also capped:
    - Number of gladiators per show (by city size)
    - Cost of gladiator upkeep and support staff
    - Tax to the imperial treasury: **33% for a free gladiator, 25% for a slave gladiator**

_(Scholarship: Michael Carter 2003, "Gladiatorial ranking and the SC de pretiis," Phoenix 57, 83–114)_

## PART 3: VENUES

### Pre-Amphitheatre Venues

- **Roman Forum / Basilica Aemilia**: earliest site for beast hunts and gladiatorial combat; gladiatorial revolts started here; Cicero reports a lion that leapt a screen and mauled two senators to death
- **Circus Maximus** (earliest form ca. 450 BC): between horse races, or in the curved _sphedon_ end — gladiatorial combats, plays, beast hunts, and public executions all took place here

### Theatre Conversions

Many Greek theatres across the empire were converted for combat. The standard gladiatorial fighting space was **ca. 22–26 metres**.

Key sites with conversions: Erythrae (for Hadrian's visit), Magnesia ad Maeandrum, Pergamum, Athens, Corinth, Dodona, Stobi, Heraclea Lyncestis, Aphrodisias, Bodrum (Turkey), and Barcino (for boxing: CIL 2.4514).

Types of conversion included:

- **Gladiatorial cage**: barriers added to protect spectators, fighting space cleared
- **Venationes** (beast hunts): high balustrade walls, enlarged oval space (as at the Theatre of Dionysos, Athens, renovated for Hadrian's visit)
- **Kolymbethra**: shallow swimming pool (water pipes and hydraulic cement confirm this at Gortyn, Crete)
- **Naumachia**: staged naval battles in flooded arenas

### The Flavian Amphitheatre (Colosseum)

- **Not called "the Colosseum" until the 4th century AD** (after repairs of AD 388)
- Built in three phases: **Vespasian** (commissioned), **Titus** (inaugurated AD 79–80), **Domitian** (added uppermost tier for women and slaves)
- Opening under Titus: **100 days** of gladiators and animals, with free food and free admission to the baths; Martial was commissioned to write a book of poems (_Liber de Spectaculis_) for the occasion
- Some amphitheatres were poorly built: the amphitheatre at **Fidenae (Italy) collapsed** during a show under Tiberius (Tacitus, _Annals_ 4.62–63)

### The Colosseum Fire (Vulcanalia)

The Colosseum burned down during the **Festival of Vulcan (23 August)** — an occasion meant to pray that harvested crops would not burn:

> _"The hunting theatre was struck by thunderbolts on the very day of the Vulcanalia... the entire upper circuit and everything in the arena was consumed... Neither human aid could avail against the conflagration, though practically every aqueduct was emptied."_ — Cassius Dio 79.25.2–3

After the fire, gladiatorial shows were held in the **Stadium of Domitian** (today's Piazza Navona) for many years.

### Naumachia Augusti

- Staged **naval battles** on a purpose-built lake (Trastevere, across the Tiber)
- Crewed by recalcitrant slaves and condemned criminals
- Water just deep enough that anyone who fell in would drown
- Crew of the **last ship standing was freed**
- Considered the site where the **greatest number of Christians were killed**; St. Stephen was martyred in the Naumachia

### Domus Flavia (Palatine)

- Audience and state dining hall added by **Domitian**
- Emperor and close associates dined on a raised dais
- Both Domitian and **Marcus Verus** (co-emperor with Marcus Aurelius) were known to favour watching **female pairs of gladiators** while dining

## PART 4: GLADIATORS

### Measuring Popularity

When assessing the cultural importance of gladiators (or any ancient spectacle), historians evaluate:

- **Context** — how many different geographic locations does evidence come from?
- **Types** — how many different kinds of objects depict it?
- **Materials** — how many different media (metal, ceramic, fresco, mosaic, coins, statues, reliefs)?
- **Durée** — how long a time period does the evidence span?
- **Location** — civilian vs. military? Roman West vs. Greek East?

### Types of Gladiators

|Type|Weapons & Armor|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|**Murmillo**|Large shield, short sword (_gladius_), heavy helmet with fish crest, greaves|Most heavily armoured; often depicted as representing "Romans"; expected to win|
|**Retiarius**|Net, trident, dagger, shoulder guard (_galerus_), minimal armor|No helmet; galerus curved up to trap opponent's sword|
|**Secutor**|Small shield, short sword, smooth-visored helmet|"Chaser"; minimal armor to enable pursuit of retiarius; helmet's smooth face had small eye holes to reduce risk of trident entering|
|**Thraex (Thracian)**|Curved sword (_sica_), small round shield (_pelta_), full helmet|Named for Thrace; often paired against murmillo|
|**Hoplomachus**|Long spear, small round shield, full helmet|Based on Greek _hoplite_ warrior|
|**Eques**|Short sword, small round cavalry shield, helmet|Fought on horseback or dismounted; only helmets as armor|
|**Iaculator**|Javelin|Ranged fighter|

> **Key rule:** Gladiators were almost always paired against a **different** type of fighter to create visual contrast and tactical interest.

### Names and Identity

Gladiators commonly used **noms de guerre** (_fighting names_) drawn from:

- Heroes and gods (e.g., Astyanax, Heracles)
- Fierce or exotic-sounding names
- Cities and regions
- Sometimes humorous names — e.g., **ILAROS** (= _Hilaros_, "good for laughs"), attested on a limestone stele from Bodrum/Halicarnassos (British Museum, AD II)

### Rules, Referees, and Outcomes

- A **referee** (_summa rudis_) was always present to manage the fight and enforce rules
- A fallen gladiator could raise a **finger of the left hand** to signal surrender/submission
- The outcome was decided by the referee, the editor (show sponsor), and/or the roar of the crowd
- **Stans missus** = "sent away standing" = a draw; both fighters helped away after combat
- **Ø or Θ (theta)** on inscriptions = _thanatos_ = dead
- A palm frond indicated victory; **laurel crowns** were reserved for _remarkable_ victories
- By law, gladiators only received a funeral stele if they **died in combat without making a sound**
- Maximum known victories for any single gladiator: **150**

### Key Inscriptions and Named Fighters

- **Astyanax vs. Kalendio** (Madrid, National Archaeological Museum mosaic): Astyanax (secutor) wins; Kalendio (retiarius) killed — "Ø"
- **Pompeii graffiti** (upcoming fights, cancelled by Vesuvius):
    - Matilius: 18 kills; L. Raecius Felix: 11 kills, 2 draws
    - ...us: 13 kills; Libanus: 64 kills
- **Borghese Gardens mosaic** (Rome, ca. AD 320): named fighters — Licentiosus, Purpureus, Sentininus, Baccibus, Asticius, Astacius/Astivus (killed), Iaculator, Rodan (killed)
- **Kourion mosaic** (Cyprus, AD III): Hoplomachus Autrasonides vs. Retiarius, Referee Dariius

### Secutor Reliefs (Baths of Diocletian, ca. 30 BC–10 BC)

- From the reign of Augustus
- Likely one secutor with images of his best kills recorded
- Inscribed M(ORTUUS) / TH(ANATOS) = dead

### Notable Artifacts

- **Galerus** (Louvre BR 1142, AD I): shoulder guard for retiarius; medallion of Heracles; curved end to trap opponent's sword
- **Pompeii gladiator barracks**: shin guards and helmets survive; Hercules depicted on right shin guard (pre-AD 79)
- **Herculaneum barracks helmets** (pre-AD 79): found in the gladiatorial barracks
- **Dagger of PRIMI, head centurion LEG III** (Naples Archaeological Museum): bone hilt, iron dagger; from Pompeii amphitheatre; sold 2020 for £370
- **Freedom token** (British Museum 1889.05.20.5): ivory (not wood — the material matters), dated **7 October AD 88**; represents _missio_ = formal discharge from gladiatorial service

## PART 5: GLADIATRICES (FEMALE GLADIATORS)

Female gladiators existed and are attested in both literary and material evidence.

### Physical Evidence

- **Lamp** (various): female gladiator dressed as an Amazon, holding small cavalry shield and double-headed axe
- **Plaque from Bodrum** (British Museum GR 1847.4-24.19, 4/4 AD I – ¼ AD II): inscription reads **APELYTHESAN AMAZON AKHILLA** = "Amazon [and] Achilla were released [from service]" — commemorates the honourable discharge of two female gladiators
- **Hamburg statuette** (Museum für Kunst und Gewerbe 1917.362): figure whose object in hand may be a curved sword (Amazon) or a strigil (bathing); gender and sport ambiguous
- **Two bronze leg greaves**: unusually, they depict women — one possibly **Omphale** (linked to the myth of gender reversal with Hercules), one **Athena as warrior**

## PART 6: COMIC BATTLES

The tradition of **mock epic** — comic or parodic combat — goes back at least to the pseudo-Homeric _Battle of Frogs and Mice_ and to images of pygmies fighting cranes on Attic black-figure vases.

In the arena this took the form of:

- **Ape as chariot-gladiator driving camels** (Rome, Baths of Diocletian, inv. 496): parody battles staged as comic relief between main events
- **Dwarf gladiators**: attested in mosaics and bronze figurines (British Museum GR 1922.7-12.4, AD I/II; hoplomachus type); literary sources include Suetonius _Life of Augustus_ 43, Statius _Silvae_ 1.6.57–64, Dio Cassius 67.8.2
- **Bull-leaping** (British Museum lamp, late AD II/early AD III): man with pole leaps over bull — a sport associated with **Minoan Crete**, apparently revived as arena entertainment

## PART 7: VENATORES (BEAST HUNTERS)

Venationes (beast hunts) were often **more popular and more widespread** than gladiatorial combat, especially in the eastern Roman world.

### Venues for Venationes

- **Theatre of Dionysos, Athens** (renovated for Hadrian): enlarged oval space, high balustrade walls — made space suitable for a pair of animals or a gladiator vs. animal
- **Bulla Regia (Tunisia)**: bear mosaic in converted orchestra — more evidence for venationes than gladiators at this site
- **Phaselia (Turkey)**: tauromachia (bull-fighting) and taurobolium (bull-leaping) attested
- **Villa Dionysos, Paphos (Cyprus)** (¼ AD II): mosaic of venator with spear vs. tiger; balustrade depicted indicates the fight took place in the orchestra of a theatre
- **Timgad**: statue of **Faustina the Younger** (wife of Marcus Aurelius) placed in the orchestra — statues were used by hunters and animals to leap around, adding to excitement; Faustina was publicly humiliated here as punishment for alleged adultery (the _Historia Augusta_ implies her son Commodus was fathered by a gladiator)

### Piazza Armerina (Sicily), AD 310–330

A remarkable mosaic sequence showing the entire supply chain of animals for the arena:

1. **Capture**: horsemen chase impala; some lions/tigers used as "bait" to draw other animals for capture (as goats still are in parts of Africa); a rider is thrown from his horse and accidentally stabs himself during the chaos
2. **Loading**: impala loaded onto ship; pockets and reinforcing patches visible on workers' clothes; one rail on ramp in "down" position; patchwork repairs on the boat
3. **Transport**: sea pine tree (upper left) indicates animals have just been taken off the ship; an unwilling auroch; a tiger watches "in some dismay" — Cicero records that fewer than **half of captured animals survived transport** from Africa to Rome
4. **Arena**: venationes in progress — horsemen, lions, and tigers in action

### Named Venatores and Events

- **Venator SERODUUS CPN vs. boar** (Köln mirror case): two military decorations (_phalerae_) with animal hunt motifs also in the case; possible connection to myth of **Cadmus** yoking wild bulls to plow Thebes
- **TAURISCUS NIKA** (Köln flask): "Tauriscus rules!"; tauriscus = "bull slayer"; venator shown wrestling a boar; bottom of lamp shows a murmillo with palm of victory
- **Antioch dining mosaic** (AD V): all figures named, some ironically — Luxurius the tiger "luxuriates" (does nothing); Romanus the tiger is killed (the Roman should be winning); Spittaria (spear man) spears Victor the tiger (who loses); Crispinus the tiger is speared simultaneously by Bullarius ("young man") and Hilarinus ("funny man")
- **Ephesus stele** (British Museum 1873.5-5.1, AD I/II): sometimes the animal wins — four consecutive fights described; at the fourth event, the venator "was carted off"; Martial wrote poems in which a venator fights multiple animals, some posed as labours of Hercules

### Mosaic Evidence (Note on Chronology)

The course raises an important question: much of the mosaic evidence for blood sports comes from **after** these activities were supposedly banned by Christian emperors. The **Daphne mosaic at Antioch** (AD V) belongs securely to Christian Antioch — one of the earliest cities to convert, where the last pagan emperor Julian died — yet it still depicts venationes enthusiastically.

## PART 8: ANIMAL VS. ANIMAL

Animal-on-animal combat was a distinct category of arena entertainment, documented across many media:

- **Basilica of Junius Bassus, Rome** (AD 331 marble _opus sectile_): tiger vs. bull — both look at the viewer; Junius Bassus was left in charge of Rome when Constantine moved to Constantinople
- **Lamps** document: bear vs. elephant; rhinoceros vs. tiger; lion vs. crocodile; lion vs. donkey

## PART 9: CONDEMNATIO AD BESTIAS (CONDEMNED TO THE BEASTS)

### Background and Origins

The practice of executing condemned criminals in the arena is **older than Rome**. A Neo-Assyrian chryselephantine plaque (British Museum, VII BC) shows a lioness mauling a Nubian captive — gold, ivory, carnelian, and lapis inlays; from the arm of a throne; reflects the triumphal ideology of Assyria during the conquest of Egypt's Second Nubian Dynasty.

### How It Worked

- Condemned criminals (_noxii_) were **cheaper than gladiators** and were therefore used as filler between main events
- Execution was staged as **entertainment** — often as mythological skits or re-enactments
- As taste for gladiatorial combat waned, demand for **public executions as spectacle** increased
- **Constantine** found he had too few condemned criminals and **doubled the number of capital crimes** to ensure enough subjects for the arena
- A famous lamp scene (ca. AD 100) depicts a **donkey penetrating a condemned female criminal (_noxia_)** — often associated with _Apuleius's Golden Ass_ (book 10), but the lamp is earlier than Apuleius; the scenario more likely references historical staged skits in which a _noxia_ played Pasiphae and was penetrated by a bull, rupturing her organs (see G.W.M. Harrison 2020)

### Christian Martyrs

Christians were particularly problematic as entertainment because **they often simply sat down and waited to die**, refusing to perform or react dramatically. Key sites:

**Ayii Deka (near Gortyn, Crete)**

- Ten Christians martyred on **23 December AD 251** in the amphitheatre of Gortyn (capital of Roman Crete)
- Icons (14th–19th century, including one once attributed to El Greco) show the martyrdom
- The _Menologion of Basil II_ (calendar of services, AD 976–1025) records the event
- **Crimes were specifically fabricated** to put the victims to death in the games — cheaper than paying for gladiators
- When dead, they were **beheaded**, heads put on public display and not buried with the bodies
- In 2003, excavations for church reconstruction found a tomb with **8 headless skeletons** of the right date, clinching the identification
- The martyr church was destroyed in the **fire of 1896** during the Cretan War of Independence

**Ignatius of Antioch**

- Popularly believed to have been martyred in the Colosseum — **this did not happen**
- Roman **citizens could not be tortured** by law
- However, **captured foreign leaders** could be: their necks were wrung in front of the Temple of Jupiter on the Capitoline during a triumph, or they were exhibited in the Colosseum
    - Martial records this for Vespasian's triumph; Constantine exhibited Alemanni (AD 305) and Franks (AD 306)
- This practice is interpreted as a form of **human sacrifice** — the last recorded sacrifice of a Gaul tied to a Greek in Rome occurred in **AD 351**

## PART 10: FUNCTIONARIES (ARENA STAFF)

### The Pompa (Procession) — Naples Museo Nazionale 6704 (AD 59)

A relief from the Pompeii amphitheatre shows the full sequence of a munera:

**Top band (pompa / opening procession):**

- Equites (horsemen)
- Weapons displayed for _probatio_ (public inspection and approval)
- Gladiators
- Editor (the person paying for the show)
- Palm fronds
- Table with patron deities of the munus
- 3 tubas
- Lictors with _fasces_ (bundles of rods = symbols of civic authority)

**Middle band (the fights):**

- Gladiators warming up with weapons
- Formal salute between two gladiators
- Noxius (condemned criminal) killed
- Gladiator submits in surrender
- _Stans missus_ (draw — fighters helped away)
- Full combat

**Bottom band (venationes):**

- Hunks of meat given away by _spintrii_ (lottery tokens used as admission or prize tokens)

### The Editor

- The editor was the person **sponsoring and paying for the show**
- A mosaic relief from Verona (AD IV/V) shows an editor standing over a fallen gladiator, about to order his throat cut
- Brescia ivory diptych of the **Lampadii** (ca. AD 430): editor with his two sons; wax tablet, presumably for recording wagers
    - **Gambling at Rome** was technically illegal _except_ for gladiatorial combat, venationes, festival games, plays (multiple endings existed for plays so people could bet on outcomes), and public celebrations of the imperial cult — effectively, gambling was legal every day

## PART 11: EQUIPMENT

### Armor and Weapons (Key Pieces)

- **Galerus** (shoulder guard, retiarius): curved end traps opponent's sword for counterattack; medallion of Heracles as patron deity (Louvre BR 1142, AD I)
- **Shin guards** (Pompeii barracks, pre-AD 79): Hercules depicted in detail on the right guard
- **Helmets** (Herculaneum barracks, pre-AD 79; German training camp): various styles preserved
- **Gladius** (short sword): the word from which "gladiator" derives; bone hilt example from Pompeii amphitheatre
- **Shields**:
    - Round shield with Medusa central boss (Naples Archaeological Museum 5669, from Pompeii amphitheatre) — decorative/ceremonial
    - Bronze shield of hoplomachus (British Museum GR 1772.3-3.141, Pompeii) — plain bronze = utilitarian fighting equipment

### Freedom Token (Missio)

- **British Museum 1889.05.20.5**: ivory (not the traditional wooden sword — the material matters as a mark of prestige); dated **7 October AD 88**
- Represents _missio_ = formal, honourable discharge from gladiatorial service
- A wooden _rudis_ (sword) was the traditional symbol of a gladiator's freedom — being presented with one meant you could retire

## SUPPLEMENTARY CONTEXT

### Why Gladiatorial Games Mattered Politically

Gladiatorial games (_munera_) were not mere entertainment — they were deeply political instruments:

- Originally held as **funeral games** to honour dead nobles; evolved into major public spectacles
- The editor gained **enormous public prestige** and political capital from hosting games
- This is why emperors carefully regulated who could host games and at what cost
- Restricting games to the **imperial cult** effectively monopolized the political benefit for the emperor alone
- The word _editor_ literally means "one who puts on [a show]" — the same root as "edition" of a book

### Gladiator Social Status

- Most gladiators were **slaves, prisoners of war, or condemned criminals**
- Some **free volunteers** (_auctorati_) also signed contracts — driven by money, glory, or desperation
- Legally, gladiators were _infames_ (stripped of civic rights) — yet they were cultural celebrities
- Gladiators had their own **barracks, doctors, and trainers** (_lanistae_); the lanista was the owner/manager of a gladiatorial troupe (_familia gladiatoria_)
- The **ludus magnus** (adjacent to the Colosseum) was the main gladiatorial training school in Rome; a system of tunnels connected it directly to the arena floor

### End of Gladiatorial Games

- Emperor **Constantine** began restricting games (AD 325), but they continued
- Theodosius I banned them in **AD 399**; effectively ended by **AD 404** after the monk Telemachus was killed trying to stop a fight — his martyrdom caused the emperor Honorius to finally suppress them
- **Venationes continued longer** than gladiatorial combat, persisting into the 6th century AD
