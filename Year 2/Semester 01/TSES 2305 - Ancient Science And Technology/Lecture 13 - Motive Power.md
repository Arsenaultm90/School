### 1. HUMAN POWER
**Key Idea:** Most power in antiquity was human muscle (often slave labor).

#### Efficiency & Mechanics
- Humans can out-walk any other species on the planet.
- Upper body acts as a pendulum → catches/releases energy efficiently.
- Gluteus maximus = most powerful muscle in the body.
- Opposable thumbs allow delicate force application.
- Humans can work in cramped conditions, rest/nourish muscles more easily.

#### Treadmill / Tread-wheel (antique)
- **Ryndham treadmill** / “hamster wheel” design.
    
- One person can lift **1 ton** on a single pulley using a tread-wheel.
    
- Used in cranes (e.g., **Tomb of the Haterii**, early AD ii).
    
    - Family owned construction business.
        
    - Relief shows 5 men inside a large tread wheel powering a crane.
        
    - Wheel can go up/down and rotate sideways.
        

#### Windlass & Hand Spikes

- **Windlass** = lifting/turning device (wells, ship’s wheels, catapults).
    
- Hand spikes more efficient than crank (crank is post-antique; needs worm gear).
    
- Mechanical advantage formula:  
    P=baQP=ab​Q  
    (P = force applied, Q = resistance, a = axle diameter, b = spike length)
    
- **Newton’s calculation**: 16 lbs per lift at 2.4 ft/min for 8 hours = 1,175,040 lbs per person (no fatigue factored).
    

#### Bicycle (invented 1817 by Baron von Drais)

- 4× speed with 1/5th the effort of walking.
    
- Response to horse shortage after Napoleonic Wars.

### ANIMAL POWER

#### Donkeys vs. Oxen

|Feature|Donkey|Ox|
|---|---|---|
|Efficiency|Lower energy needs, coarse hay|Most efficient beast of burden|
|Work duration|–|2.5 hrs without break, ~4 hrs sleep|
|Digestion|Sensitive to mold|Multi-stage stomach, high nutrition recovery|
|Water needs|10–25 L/day|–|
|Plowing|–|~½ football field/day = 1 _iugum_|

- **Donkey mill** (e.g., Ostia): animal walks in circle, turns vertical axle.
    
- **Oxen** gave name to _boustrophedon_ (turn plow like ox).
    

#### Limitations

- Draft animals consume more food relative to energy than humans.
    
- Horses not sure-footed on uneven land.
    
- Donkeys/mules preferred for mills.

### WATER POWER

#### General Principles

- **Gravity flow** is basis of all vertical wheels.
    
- Gradient controls speed of flow.
    
- Water used first and foremost for **irrigation**.
    

#### Types of Vertical Waterwheels

|Type|Efficiency|Description|
|---|---|---|
|**Undershot**|20%|Low/no gradient; relies on momentum; prey to summer drying|
|**Overshot**|70%|Preferred in antiquity; needs 15′ drop; powered by speed + weight|
|**Breast-shot**|50%|Water enters at axle level; less stress; rare in antiquity|
|**Pitch-back** (reverse overshot)|90%|Used for pumping mines; needs iron/steel|

#### Historical Development

- **Bucket mill** (London, Gresham St, AD ii) – earliest type.
    
- **Vertical wheel** – ½ iii BC Byzantium.
    
- **Horizontal wheel** – c. 240 BC Alexandria (Apollonius of Perge).
    
- **Grist mill** – by 71 BC Pontus (Strabo 12.3.30).
    
- **Water-powered sawmill** – AD iii, Hierapolis (Turkey), with connecting rods.
    

#### Barbegal Mill (near Arles, AD ii)

- 16 overshot wheels; largest known ancient mill operation.
    
- Fed by aqueduct from above.
    
- Discovered by A. Trevor Hodge.
    
- Remains: holes in bedrock, hydraulic cement, polished stone from water flow.
    
- Wood/metal gone; _anastylosis_ reconstruction.
    

#### Other Sites

- **Baths of Caracalla** (Rome): overshot wheels for grain mills for restaurants.
    
- **Janiculum** (Rome): undershot wheels fed by _Aqua Traiana_.
    
- **Nahal Taninim** (Israel): double horizontal grist mill, AD ii.
    
- **Chemtou** (Algeria, AD 114): first true turbine (vertical axle, angled blades).
    
- **Hama (Syria)**: 17 norias (bucket wheels), destroyed 2014.

### WATER LIFTING & IRRIGATION DEVICES

#### Shaduf (counterpoise lift)

- Long pole on fulcrum; weight on land side.
    
- Easier to pull counterweight down (legs + body weight) than lift full bucket.
    
- Examples: Thebes (Egypt), Berlin pelike c. 500 BC.
    

#### Persian Wheel (Sakieh)

- Invented India iii BC; in Egypt by ii BC.
    
- Jugs tied to wheel; water released before top to reduce weight.
    
- Powered by oxen moving in circle.
    

#### Archimedes’ Screw

- Possibly improved by Archimedes (†212 BC) with a sleeve.
    
- Earlier cuneiform tablet of Sennacherib (704–681 BC).
    
- Used for Hanging Gardens of Babylon (Strabo).
    
- Still used for pumping sewage.
    

#### Piston Pumps

- Invented by **Ctesibius** (c. 250 BC) for water organ.
    
- Modified by **Hero** for fire engine.
    
- Required water under pressure; ancient pipes often couldn’t withstand it.

### CANALS, TUNNELS, AQUEDUCTS

#### Diolkos (Corinth Canal)

- 8 km long, vi BC.
    
- Ships pulled by humans on sledges/rollers.
    
- Completed 1896.
    

#### Qanat (underground canals)

- Used mainly for irrigation.
    
- Water moves by gravity from hillsides.
    

#### Famous Tunnels

|Tunnel|Date|Length|Features|
|---|---|---|---|
|Siloam (Jerusalem)|701 BC (?)|533 m|Dug from both ends; 0.6° gradient|
|Eupalinos (Samos)|vi BC|1036 m|0° gradient; met within 3 cm; ventilation shafts|

#### Roman Aqueduct System

- _Aqua_ (water) + _ducere_ (to lead).
    
- Components: source → tunnels → bridges (_substructio_) → fountain/reservoir.
    
- Gradient 3°–6° essential for gravity flow.
    
- **Porta Maggiore** (Rome): carries 3 aqueduct lines.
    
- **Gadara** (Jordan): 170 km, longest in antiquity; started by Hadrian AD 129.
    

#### Pipes & Materials

|Material|Properties|
|---|---|
|Ceramic|Overwhelming majority; socketed; replaceable; laid in sand/straw for earthquakes|
|Lead|Rolled from sheet (Vitruvius 8.6.1); imperial stamps; used where pressure high|
|Stone collar|Shock absorber; vibrates in earthquake, pipe inside stays still|

#### Lead Poisoning Awareness

- Vitruvius 8.6.10-11 recommended ceramic over lead when possible.
    
- Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) coating on pipes reduces leaching.
    
- Flint, Michigan case: corrosive water removed coating → lead poisoning.

### WATER DISTRIBUTION & USE IN CITIES

#### Terminus of Aqueducts

- Mostly **baths**.
    
- One aqueduct ended at _naumachia_ (mock sea battles, used to kill Christians).
    
- Small fountains every ~6 blocks in Rome.
    

#### Cisterns

- **Cistern of Justinian** (Istanbul): 138×64.6×9 m; 80,000 m³ capacity; 336 marble columns, some with Medusa bases.
    

#### Fountains & Spigots

- **Spili (Crete)** : 52 lion-head spouts.
    
- Spigots = on/off (no flow regulation in antiquity).
    
- Lead spigots from Bath (London) and Himera (Sicily).
    

#### Indoor Plumbing

- Wealthy homes had pipes inside walls (rudimentary air conditioning).
    
- **House of the Vestals** (Pompeii): bath + 3 cisterns.
    
- **House of the Vettii** (Pompeii): impluvium jets, shut-off valves, stack pipes.

### SEWERS, DRAINS, LATRINES

#### General

- Fewer taboos about body functions.
    
- Most sewers were **open**, ran along streets, shallow.
    
- Underground sewers only in largest cities.
    

#### Key Sites

|Site|Feature|
|---|---|
|Tiryns (pre-1200 BC)|Open sewer at workers’ quarters|
|Ostia (AD ii)|Public latrine, open to both genders, no stalls|
|Athens Roman _forum_ (AD ii)|Public latrine|
|**Cloaca Maxima** (Rome)|Drained Forum swamp (c. 600 BC); arch from 222 BC visible; exit to Tiber|
|Corinth (AD 50s)|Sewer on Lechaion road|

#### Drainage Components

- Stack pipes from roof to sewer (ceramic or lead).
    
- Water towers (Herculaneum) with tanks on top.
    
- _Puteal_ (raised well head) for hand-over-hand bucket drawing.
    

#### “Loo” etymology

- _Gardez lou!_ = “watch out below” (when throwing chamber pot out window).

### AUTOMATA & GEARS

- **Automaton** = any machine where one action triggers a series of others (e.g., flushing toilet).
    
- Required wooden gears (none survive).
    
- Examples:
    
    - Bull of Phalaris (acoustic torture device).
        
    - Solomon’s throne (animals sat up/made noise).
        
    - Trimalchio’s dinner (Petronius).
        
- **Hero of Alexandria** (c. 10 BC – AD 70): non-productive inventions (steam engine, wind organ).

### STEAM & WIND POWER

#### Hero’s Steam Engine

- Non-productive; no pistons/valves.
    
- Demonstrated principle only.
    
- Impractical: needed stronger materials, lubricants, ball bearings, nozzles, coal.
    
- Minimum 1500 RPM beyond ancient material limits.
    

#### Wind Power

- **Earliest non-human/animal power** = water mills (c. 400 BC Persia?).
    
- Antipater of Paros: “Persian girls use sails to grind grain.”
    
- **Hero’s wind-driven pipe organ** – likely a concept toy.
    
- **Nishtafun** (wind-driven grist mill): credited to Abū Lo’lo’a (AD 644), who assassinated the caliph.
    
- Lasithi (Crete): 19th-century windmills, same tech as water mills.
    

#### Ships

- Preferred power = wind + currents.
    
- Votive ship sculpture, Baths of Diocletian.
