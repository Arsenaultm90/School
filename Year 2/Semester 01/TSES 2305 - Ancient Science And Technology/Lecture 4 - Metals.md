# Technology and Risk (Framing Concept)

#### Key Idea
> **There are no industrial accidents without industry**
- New technologies introduce **new risks**
- Skeletal evidence allows archaeologists to:
    - Identify professions
    - Assess technological intensity
- Examples:
    - Finger arthritis → weaving or repetitive crafts
    - Worn teeth → leather chewing (softening hides)

#### Modern Parallel (Used as Analogy)
- Industrial accidents today:
    - Thousands of deaths and injuries annually
- Shows that **technological progress always has human cost**
- Same principle applies to antiquity

# Metals in the Stone Age

#### Metals Known in the Stone Age
All major metals were **known**:
- Gold
- Silver
- Copper
- Tin
- Iron

#### What Was Missing
- **Processing knowledge**, not raw materials
- Hence:
    - Stone Age ≠ ignorance
    - Stone Age = lack of **kiln & smelting technology**

#### Ennos’ Argument
- Better termed the **“Tree Age”**
- Most tools were for:
    - Cutting
    - Shaping
    - Dressing wood

# Metals and Melting Points

> **Metals are tied directly to kiln technology**
Knowing melting points explains **why** some metals appear earlier than others.




---
# Gold

#### Properties
- Concentrates naturally into:
    - Flakes
    - Nuggets
- Melting point: **1064°C**
- Extremely soft in pure form (24 carat)
    - Not useful for tools or weapons
    - Modern jewelry usually 10–12 carat

#### Extraction
- Does **not always require smelting**
- Can be:
    - Pounded free from rock
    - Collected from water

#### Sheep Skin Method
- Lanolin in sheep skin attracts gold particles
- Possible origin of:
    - **Golden Fleece**
    - Gold lamb in myth of Atreus & Thyestes




---
# Iron

#### Naturally Occurring Iron
- Usually from **meteorites**
- Example: Hoba meteorite (Namibia)

#### Properties
- Melting point: **1538°C**
- Too high for:
    - Stone Age
    - Copper Age
    - Bronze Age

#### Iron Age
- True iron smelting only possible **c. 750 BC**
- Requires advanced furnace technology


---
# Terrestrial Iron

- Naturally mixed with **nickel**
- Still not workable without high-temperature furnaces



---
# Smelting (Core Process)

#### Definition
- **Smelting** = heating ore to burn off impurities, leaving metal behind

#### Open-Pit Smelting
- Earliest evidence:
    - **Lead**, c. 6500 BC
    - Çatal Höyük (Turkey)



---
# Lead, Tin, and Silver

#### Melting Points
- Lead: **327.5°C**
- Tin: **231°C**
- Silver: **962°C**

> Frying pans reach ~350°C → lead and tin melt easily

#### Important Rule
> **Only lead and tin can be smelted in a wood fire**

#### Silver Extraction
- Silver often exists as **silver oxide**
- Heating lead ore:
    - Lead melts off
    - Leaves beads of silver behind

#### Crucial Observation
- **No evidence** that Stone Age people exploited this process
- Even though they had:
    - The ores
    - Fire
    - The temperatures

#### Why Not?
- They did not conceptualize the process
- Toxic fumes (including cyanide) likely killed experimenters
- Early failures may have discouraged repetition


---
# The Copper Age

#### Copper Properties
- Lower smelting temperature than iron
- Does not require bellows initially

#### Copper Furnaces
- Example: **Ayn Sokhna (Egypt)**
    - No air holes for bellows
    - Heat sufficient without forced air

#### Kiln Design
- Chimney:
    - Improves heat capture
    - Cools ash before exit
    - Prevents accidental fires

#### Geography
- Ayn Sokhna:
    - On route between Nile and Red Sea
    - Major **transshipment hub**



---
# Chronology of Copper Metallurgy

- Earliest copper smelting:
    - **Belovode (Serbia)**, c. **5500–5000 BC**
- Earliest copper object:
    - Axe head from **Çan Hasan (Turkey)**, c. **5000 BC**
- Regional adoption:
    - Sumer & Elam: before 4000 BC
    - Egypt: later
    - Greece: much later

> Metallurgical knowledge spreads **unevenly**, not simultaneously

# Summary

- “All major metals were known in the Stone Age, but not processed.”
- “Only lead and tin can be smelted in a wood fire.”
- “Iron metallurgy requires furnace technology not available until the Iron Age.”
- “Technological failure can be fatal and may halt innovation.”
- “Metallurgy follows kiln technology, not raw material availability.”
