
# Horticulture Is a Technology

> Agriculture is not just planting — it is **soil management over time**

Key constraints:
- Soil nutrients are **finite**
- Labor is mostly **manual**
- Most farms are **small**
- Bad choices → famine and collapse



---
# Choosing the Field (Greek Case Study)

#### Early Greece
- **Annual planting** in same field
- Result:
    - Soil nutrient depletion
    - Agricultural collapse
    - Survivors forced to move and replant elsewhere


#### Greece by Hesiod (Archaic Period)
**Fallow System**
- Field planted and harvested **1 year**
- Left fallow **2 years**
- At any time:
    - **½ the land not producing**
- Consequence:
    - To farm 25 hectares → must own **50 hectares**

> Land ownership becomes a survival requirement


#### Greece by Classical Period (Post-480 BC)
**Alternating Crops**
- One year grain
- Next year **legumes** (nitrogen-fixing)

Common legumes:
- Alfalfa
- Clover
- Peas
- Beans
- Lentils

**Why Legumes Matter**
- Fabaceae have **root nodules**
- Nodules produce **ammonia**
- Converts into **ammonium**
- Essential for **amino acid formation** in soil

> This is the biological basis of modern fertilizers

# Crop Rotation

- Ancient systems ≠ modern crop rotation
- **Modern crop rotation** developed in **1730s**
    - Charles Viscount Townshend
- Ancient systems were:
    - Empirical
    - Partial
    - Not scientifically formalized

# Israel: Religious Agriculture (Shmita)

#### Shmita (שמיטה)
- Every **7th year**:
    - Field left fallow
    - Agricultural debts cancelled
- Based on:
    - Leviticus 25:1–7
    - Torah and Haftorah traditions

#### Great Sabbath
- Every **49th year**
- All agriculture suspended

#### Economic Support
- Temple taxes saved
- Used to pay allowances during non-production years

#### Land Use Impact
- At any time:
    - **1/7 of land not producing**
- Today:
    - ~50% of Israeli farmers still observe shmita

# Environmental Assumptions (Eastern Mediterranean)

- Assumption:
    - More arid land → fewer nutrients absorbed

- Problem:
    - **Crete is as arid as Levant**
    - But does **not** follow same agricultural pattern
        
- Conclusion:
    - Climate alone does not explain agricultural strategy

#### Implication
> Agricultural productivity was limited by human endurance

# Fertilizing in the Ancient Mediterranean

#### A. Manure
- **Not regularly used** as fertilizer
- Primary uses:
    - Fuel for cooking
    - Heating
    - Firing pottery
- Bird fertilizers:
    - Not used until **1880s**
- Human waste:
    - Used sometimes
    - Causes stomach illness
    - Cicero refers to “Roman tummy”


#### B. Turning Crops Under (Green Manure)
- Barley fields (Israel, c. **8000 BC**)
- Archaeological evidence shows:
    - Barley planted **specifically to be plowed under**
- Nitrogen fixation without harvesting
- Modern parallel:
    - Clover or rape grown and turned under


#### C. Composting
- Possible in Egypt:
    - Chaff separated from wheat may have been composted
- Roman evidence:
    - Varro (_De Re Rustica_ 1.55)
    - Describes composting
    - Calls it a **“lazy man’s option”**


#### D. Volcanic Marl
- Volcanic soils rich in:
    - Calcium
    - Phosphates
- Used near volcanic regions (e.g., Crete)


#### E. Wood Ash
- Ash collected after fires
- Spread during planting
- Provides:
    - Limited potassium
- Modern parallel:
    - Sub-Saharan Africa
    - Fields burned every third year for pot ash

# Why Fertilizer Development Was Limited

> The main brake on fertilizer development was **farm size**

- Most farms:
    - Too small
    - Too labor-limited

- No surplus labor to:
    - Transport manure
    - Manage compost at scale

- Fertilization remained:
    - Opportunistic
    - Low-intensity




---
# Summary 

- “Soil exhaustion caused early agricultural collapse.”
- “At any given time, much land was deliberately not in production.”
- “Nitrogen fixation by legumes was understood empirically, not chemically.”
- “Manure was more valuable as fuel than fertilizer.”
- “Agricultural systems reflect labor limits as much as knowledge.”
