
> **Opening principle:** The lecture opens with an inscribed nail from the Sanctuary of Hera at Argos (pre-500 BC, Nafplio Archaeological Museum). _"If a nail can have magic, anything and everything can have magic."_ The nail's flat head and square section are inscribed with a curse — normally curses were written on papyrus and nailed to a wall, but here the nail itself carries the text.

## FRAMING: WHAT IS "MAGIC"?

In the ancient world the distinction between magic, religion, and medicine was far less sharp than it is today. Key characteristics of ancient magic:

- Much of it **included obscenity** as a means of averting the evil eye
- Much of it included **deliberate deception** to prevent those who might wish to undo or break a spell from being able to read or understand it (→ _ephesia epigrammata_, see below)
- Magic was **condoned in early Christianity**: repeating prayers attributed to the Virgin Mary in childbirth was thought to help with childbirth and could even dissolve the chains of Christians captured by pagans; this contributed to belief in the miracle power of icons of martyrs
- Only in the **second century AD** does magic become a sinister force in some Christian contexts — the first "heresy" is that of Simon Magus (Simon the Sorcerer), founder of the Gnostics
- **Execration texts** (spells against enemies) continue among Coptic Christians and Muslims into at least the **eleventh century AD**, where they are considered to express the will of God

The word **"magus"** (plural: _magi_) is the name for a Zoroastrian priest serving the Medes (peak affluence 678–549 BC) and Persians (peak affluence 550–332 BC). The "Magi" of the Christian New Testament are not "kings" — on the etymology of the name they are **astrologers**.

## PART 1: AMULETS

### What Is an Amulet?

Amulets are **apotropaic** — designed to avert the evil eye and protect the wearer. Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans were all deeply superstitious. Amulets could be:

- Dedicated to a deity
- Worn on the person (necklaces, bracelets, rings)
- Carried on the person
- Buried with the deceased

### What Makes an Amulet More Potent?

The potency of an amulet was believed to increase with:

1. **Material** — the more expensive, the better; ivory always important; silver was actually more costly than gold for Egyptians
2. **Colour** — blue is best; red second best
3. **Scarcity** of material
4. **Number of amulets** — amulets were considered alive and fed strength from each other
5. **Number of images** on an amulet — more images = more powerful
6. **Spells carved** on the amulet — especially prayers to Osiris

### Egyptian Amulets (NY Metropolitan Museum collection)

**Heart amulet**: The heart was mummified and returned to the body. In the test of the dead, the heart must remain intact to be weighed against the feather of Ma'at (divine truth). Egyptians were often buried with **multiple heart amulets** that could be activated by spells if the heart was eaten.

**Scarab**: The second most important Egyptian amulet.

- Symbol of Re as the deity who returns the sun from darkness to dawn
- The animal (dung beetle) that in Egyptian religion conveyed the deceased to underworld judgement
- Became a symbol of successfully passing the tests and being under the protection of Re

**Sa-amulet**: Represents the soul — specifically the soul of a person who has passed the test for immortality.

**Isis-knot (Tit)**: Placed on the mummy of someone who had been an official in the cult of Isis; Isis was compelled (in Egyptian belief) to help any former official of hers attain eternal life.

**Djed**: Altar associated with Osiris — god and judge of the dead.

**Wdjet eye (Eye of Re/Horus)**: Associated with the sun that always returns — a promise of eternal life. By magic spell, a Wdjet eye painted on a sarcophagus allowed the deceased to **watch their own funeral**.

**Bes**: Deity of dirty jokes and childbirth; wears a lettuce crown (symbol of sexual prowess); long, never-erect penis; stance resembles someone defecating (which carried strong magic); originally female, became male in Egyptian belief; equated by Greek Ptolemaic rulers with Silenus, king of the satyrs.

**Baboon with Wdjet eye**: Avatar of Re; in Egyptian tradition the first animal to greet the dawn — the noise of baboons at dawn drives away the evil eye.

**Hedgehog**: Especially popular in the Old Kingdom; known for surviving at the edge of the cultivatable land; its return in spring after hibernation = eternal life.

**Fish**: Most potent if made in gold and turquoise; powerful because fish can swim upside down; associated with Hatmehyt, goddess of regeneration of the Nile; considered always happy, youthful, and supple.

**Bastet/Sekhmet**: Originally two separate lioness warrior goddesses; merged (syncretized) in the XXII (Libyan) Dynasty (945–715 BC) into a single lioness deity who protects the deceased like a warrior fighting on their behalf — but only if you worship her.

**Anubis**: Led the deceased to judgement before Osiris; could be bribed (or compelled by spells) to tip the balance of the scales of judgement in one's favour.

**Taweret**: Hippopotamus fertility goddess; associated with childbirth, child nurture, and taming chaos in the home. A second, dangerous hippopotamus deity existed that wished to eat the deceased's heart — knowing the difference between the two was a test of one's spiritual preparation.

**Apotropaic wands** (British Museum and NY Met, ca. 1250 BC): Now believed to be used to help with childbirth; whenever possible made in elephant ivory; protective symbols include Bes urinating, Taweret with knives, Nekhbet the vulture, lionesses, winged demons, and crocodiles.

### Pectoral of Tutankhamun (Cairo, Egyptian Museum, ca. 1340–1322 BC)

Filled with protective symbolism: Nekhbet vulture wings, multiple sun discs of Re, winged scarab, numerous snakes, Isis and Nephthys, Prayer to Osiris (Book of the Dead, prayer 151).

### Tomb of Henutmehyt (XXVI / Nubian Dynasty, 664–525 BC, British Museum)

- 20 amulets across the chest; amulets on sides of arms and around the head
- Canopic urns in each corner of the tomb; held the four principal internal organs (brain, liver, kidneys, lungs), each in a jar with an image of its divine protector
- Middle of each wall: **shabti** (figure of deceased animated to perform services for eternity), **djed** (reverence to Osiris), **papyrus** (navigation spells for the underworld), **Anubis** (guide and guardian)
- The misnamed "Canopic" urns were so named because the first archaeologists to record them found them at Canopus

### Syncretic / Late Antique Amulets

- **Nea Paphos (Cyprus), AD V/VI**: Once encased in gold; obverse is a palindrome that reads "Yahweh is the bearer of the secret name, the lion of Re, secure in his shrine" — a curious mix of mystical Judaism with Egyptian burial cult; reverse shows Harpocrates, sun, planets, mummified Osiris; discovered 2011
- **Jerusalem Bible Lands Museum, AD V**: Soap stone amulet pierced to wear around neck; Christ raises Lazarus on one side; three lines of _ephesia epigrammata_ in pseudo-Greek on the reverse
- **Private collection, AD V**: Soft black stone; Abraham turns away from the sacrifice of Isaac; four lines of pseudo-Hebrew _ephesia epigrammata_ on the reverse

## PART 2: THE BULLA

### What Is a Bulla?

The bulla (Latin: "ball-shaped") was an **Etruscan tradition** brought to Rome. It was an apotropaic charm worn by male children:

- Given to a male child at **9 days old**
- Worn on the neck as an amulet until the **14th birthday**
- At Rome, the coming-of-age ceremony involved dedicating the bulla (and a lock of hair) to **Jupiter Optimus Maximus Capitolinus** on the Capitoline
- This ceremony marked the point at which a male could marry, own property, and begin military training (not yet service)
- Suetonius records the ceremony of **Nero** (aged 14, AD 51): he also held a scroll representing his maiden speech to the Senate granting Troy tax-free status

### Material and Decoration

- Generally lead, associated with death and the underworld (for its apotropaic powers)
- Wealthier people coated lead with **gold**, but lead remained the core
- Sometimes decorated with the **Gorgoneion** (decapitated head of Medusa) — in myth, looking at her could turn one to stone; her killed-monster status was thought to retain and project protective power
- Literary evidence suggests charms in the accompanying pouch were often **penis-shaped** (like the modern Italian _cornetto_ charm)

### Who Wore a Bulla?

- Male children: universally from 9 days to 14 years old
- **Officials during their term of office** and **generals at their triumphs** wore a bulla
- **Deities** could be depicted wearing a bulla as a symbol of their divinity — in that context they confer apotropaic power rather than receive it (example: Amphitrite mosaic, Bardo Museum, Tunis, 2/2 AD IV)
- **Women**: because at Roman and Greek law the legal status of women at all points in their lives was the same as that of slaves and boys under 14, women could (and did) wear a bulla their entire lives (confirmed by a relief, Baths of Diocletian, AD II)
- Whether pre-pubescent girls wore bullae remains controversial

## PART 3: PHALLIC APOTROPAIC OBJECTS

The **phallus** was one of the most powerful and widespread apotropaic symbols in the ancient world. Its power derived from the idea that anything transgressive, obscene, or physically extreme would startle and repel the evil eye.

### Key Principles

- **Dwarfism and non-conventional body types** were apotropaic — the unusual body startles the evil eye
- **Large penis** = apotropaic (not erotic in this context)
- **Grotesque figures** (Silenus, Nubian dwarfs, polyphallic composites) repel the evil eye through sheer ugliness
- **Bells and noise** also scared off the evil eye
- **Wings** on phallic objects stressed the ability to move quickly to intercept evil

### Tintinnabula (Shop Bells)

Tintinnabula were phallic wind-chimes hung on shop doors to avert the evil eye and thieves:

- **Herculaneum, I BC** (Naples): large penis, penis in shape of a raging lion, dwarf in military costume, bells
- **Pompeii, I BC** (Naples): polyphallic; bells (now missing); erect penis; money bag (done as parody of Hermes/Mercury, god of merchants and equally god of thieves)
- **Flying phalloi** (Terme Museum, Rome): composite monsters with animal forequarters, legs, and tail all ending as penises; woman rider opening herself to "vaginal entry" by the tail-phallus; suggestion of bestiality
- The most common form: winged anthropomorphic figure riding a large penis, with goat (not horse) legs (randy reputation), bells attached, and the figure doing something rude

### Doorways and Liminal Spaces

Any **liminal space** (transition from one place to another) carried the risk of the evil eye:

- **Ceramic floor tile** (Naples, from Pompeii): shape of penis and testicles; inscribed _HIC HABITAT FELICITAS_ = "here lives happiness"
- **Macrophallic doorposts** (Naples, from Pompeii): just outside the door; limestone; bore the name of the house's owner — essentially an address plaque in the shape of a penis
- **Stele in shape of a small shrine** (Naples, from Pompeii): pediment, walls, and columns — where one would expect an image of a deity, there is a penis with testicles

### House of the Vettii, Pompeii

One of the richest concentrations of phallic apotropaic imagery in Pompeii:

- **Garden**: macrophallic statue in the pose of an athlete (pees instead of throwing a spear); fountain with a naked boy holding grapes and a dove (all with erotic symbolism); spout of fountain through the mouth of the dove
- **Kitchen fresco**: Priapus, god of gardens, wearing a Persian-style hat; holds a scale with his penis on one side balanced against a bag of produce; basket of ripe fruit below

### Other Phallic Amulets

- **Ring with phallus** (Littlehampton Museum, West Sussex, AD 43–212): ca. 14 cm. diameter; worn by a child; from near the Roman village of Angmering
- **Oxford Ashmoleon scyphos** (2/2 V BC): exterior has staring eyes reflecting the evil eye back at the viewer; base in shape of testicles and penis; interior with Medusa
- **Penis-shaped oil flask** (Corinth): used to carry bath oil to the gymnasium — any place with potential danger (robbery, sporting defeat, sexual harassment) needed apotropaic help

## PART 4: THE EVIL EYE

### How the Evil Eye Worked

The evil eye (_baskania_ in Greek) was the belief that envy, malice, or simply an admiring gaze could cause misfortune. Defences included:

- **Staring eyes** on objects that reflect the evil eye back at the viewer
- **Obscene or grotesque images** that startle and repel
- **Noise** (bells, clangers) that frightens it away
- **Dark, empty spaces** were thought to attract the evil eye — it preferred corners of rooms; this is why ancient Greek art has a _horror vacui_ (horror of empty spaces) — every surface filled with decoration so the evil eye cannot gain purchase
- **Inversions and deceptions**: upside-down objects, images of objects on the objects themselves, and deliberate gibberish all confuse and deter the evil eye (which was considered "none too bright")

### Evil Eye Amulet (London Bloomberg Excavation, ca. AD 60s)

- Copper-gold alloy brooch with blue enamel in the form of an evil eye
- Found in the Roman fort at Londinium; dates to the revolt of Boudicca
- Comparable to the Mexican _Ojo de Dios_ wall amulet

## PART 5: DEMON BOWLS

Babylonian demon bowls belong to a specific region (Mesopotamia) and time (the transition from Christianity to Islam, roughly AD VI–VII). All communities in the area — Jewish, Christian, Zoroastrian, Muslim — appear to have adopted the practice.

### Form and Function

- Inscribed earthenware bowls
- Scripts: pseudo-Syriac, pseudo-Greek, pseudo-Hebrew, pseudo-Pehlevi, pseudo-Mandaic (all in pseudo-Aramaic-style cursive)
- Script almost always spiral toward the centre or in concentric rings
- The bowl was most often **buried face down** — the demon has been captured
- Sometimes two bowls were glued together with pitch; occasionally inscribed eggshell or **fragments of human skull** were placed inside (early Egyptian rulers drank brains from skulls of conquered leaders)
- Often placed in **corners of rooms** to trap demons; could also cover holes in walls
- Could be buried with a deceased person (to protect them), or placed by an enemy's house and broken to release demons onto them
- Considered the **precursor to Venetian bacini** (decorative bowls set into building facades)

### Eggs in Magic

Eggs appear frequently in ancient magic across many contexts:

- One of the most frequent products of a spell or left as a gift to empower one in (the eggshell would have been intact with the egg sucked out)
- In **Orphism**: the central image of the creation of the universe — a snake is often shown coiled around an egg or eating one
- Eggshells found in demon bowls
- Eggs left at tombs by women as food for the dead
- Often mentioned in **curse tablets** as being smashed when making a curse
- Mentioned in **anti-erotic spells** (spells by women to counteract love spells cast on them)
- Eggshell could have a curse written on it and be deposited in the grave of someone one disliked

## PART 6: BLOOMBERG EXCAVATION AMULETS (London)

The Roman fort at Londinium (modern Bloomberg Headquarters site) was built by Claudius during the invasion (AD 43); destroyed by Boudicca (AD 61); rebuilt AD 65; abandoned as a garrison in the early V century. At least 100% certainty that **Claudius, Petronius, and Trajan** stayed here. Most amulets date to the revolt of Boudicca (ca. AD 60s).

### Key Finds

**Mogontius tablet** (ca. AD 65–80): Oak stick inscribed LONDINION MOGONIO — "In London, to Mogons" (Celtic warrior deity). Thrown into the River Walbrook (preserved by cold and pressure). A Roman soldier showing reverence to a Celtic deity — whose name he Latinized from "Mogons" to "Mogontius." Once thrown in the river, the projected magic could not be undone. The city of Mainz (Germany) was originally named _Mogontinum_ = "place sacred to Mogons."

**Bone amulet**: Human bones were thought most magically potent; hole in centre for a string or thong; left end carved as a penis; right end as a thumb sticking out between the first two fingers — imitation of a penis emerging from the foreskin.

**Horse bridle amulet**: Copper-gold alloy (alloys require more work and are therefore more potent); clanger to ward off evil eye (and also to prevent the horse from being skittish); hand gesture of thumb between first two fingers.

**Gladius (sword) amulet**: Small sword with hole, awarded for being wounded on the front of the body in battle; riveted to a breastplate. Gladiatorial imagery was considered especially potent.

**Gladiator helmet amulet**: Thracian-mask type — the most respected type of gladiator. Pierced with a hole for suspension.

## PART 7: LAMELLAE (WISH TABLETS AND ORPHIC GOLD LEAVES)

A _lamella_ (plural: _lamellae_) is a thin metal sheet — usually gold, silver, or bone — inscribed with prayers, magic formulas, or instructions for the deceased. **Not all lamellae are Totenpass** (_German_: passport for the dead); some are curses, some are prayers for protection in life.

### Why Gold?

- Gold reserved for what one wished positively
- Generally placed on top of the chest cavity of the deceased
- Normally restricted to **religions that promised an afterlife** — Dionysiac/Orphic worship, and some Egyptian deities (rare and late)
- Most important treaties were ratified in gold to stress their importance (exception: the Hittite–Egyptian peace treaty of 1259 BC was done in silver, because silver was more costly for Egyptians)

### Orphic Lamellae — Distribution

- **Bone lamellae**: all from the V BC (400s BC); Greek colonies in grain-rich areas on the north coast of the Black Sea; the bone itself adds to the power (human bone more potent; bone of a killed enemy most potent of all)
- **Gold lamellae**: almost all from Greece and Greek colonies; distribution distinct from bone lamellae — the two types are geographically mutually exclusive; Orphism continues into at least AD VI

### Orphic Bone Lamellae from Olbia (St. Petersburg, State Hermitage Museum)

**OTF 463**: BIOS THANATOS BIOS ALETHEIA | AS | DIORPHIKOI

- _Bios Thanatos = Life Death_; _Bios Aletheia = True Life_ → "True life in death"
- _Dios_ = Dionysos, Orphic god of immortality
- Orphic pairs of opposites imply one another: death implies life after death

**OTF 464**: EIRENE POLEMOS | ALETHEIA PSEUDOS | DION[YSOS] | A

- _Eirene Polemos = Peace War_; _Aletheia Pseudos = Truth Lie_; _Dion_ = abbreviation of Dionysos

### _Ephesia Epigrammata_ (Deliberately Unintelligible Text)

A crucial concept throughout magic: inscriptions that are **partially or wholly gibberish on purpose** to confuse those who might wish to undo the magic. Named for their significant use at the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus, where they were scrawled on the cult statue or placed on tablets in front of it. Potency is increased by having letters **stamped from a punch** rather than inscribed by hand.

### Key Lamella Examples

**Oslo, Schøyen Collection MS 5236 (VI BC)**: Oldest surviving lamella (not a Totenpass); dedicated to Apollo the archer; likely a request to defeat enemies; some text deliberately unintelligible; potency increased by punch-stamping; unusual evidence of **semen on the statue** to increase potency of prayer.

**Getty Museum 1975.7194 (Orphic lamella)**: Prayer of a devotee of Orphism acknowledging his spiritual thirst and asking for translation to the Orphic afterlife: _"I (masculine) am parched with thirst and am dying — but grant me to drink from the ever-flowing spring. On the right is a white cypress. 'Who are you? Where are you from?' I am a son of Earth and starry sky. But my race is heavenly."_ _(Translation: Graf and Johnston, 2007)_

**Getty Museum OTF 484 (epistomion from Thessaly)**: _Epistomion_ = rolled up and placed in the mouth of the deceased (_epi_ = in; _stoma_ = mouth). First three lines indicate the deceased has been initiated into Orphic mysteries; last three lines indicate what he will receive.

**Amphipolis lamella of Archeboule** (Thessaloniki Archaeological Museum, ca. 300 BC): _"I am the devoted priest of Dionysos Bachios, Archeboule — given in expectation of return."_ The word _antidoron_ (anti = expectation of exchange + doron = gift) implies a deal with the god — Greek, Roman, and Egyptian religion operated on the assumption that gods could be bargained with, and Egyptians believed they could even be **compelled**.

**Billingford lamella** (Norfolk Museum, post-AD 60): A soldier from a Roman fort on the German border, posted to northern England after Boudicca's revolt. Invoking Iao (=Yahweh) and Abrasax in Greek. Unusually, names his **mother** rather than his father (she may have been an official in a cult or regarded as having magical powers). Fewer than **70 gold lamellae** to Dionysos and Orpheus are known to survive.

**Fayum portrait mummy (AD II)**: Gold lamella rolled up and placed on a leather thong; worn as protection in life; worn in death to ensure the deity remembers to escort the deceased to eternal life.

### Orphic Lamellae in Italy

**Naples, Museo Nazionale OTF 488 (V BC)**: The deceased will be "enrolled among those with immortality" (_athanatoi_ = a + Thanatos = "not dead"); lines 6–14 describe what he expects to do in the next world; longer lamellae can contain **acrostics and anagrams** as magical formulas.

**Valentina, Museo Archaeologico OTF 474 (V BC)**: Epistomion of a Greek colonist; some formulas list a long catalogue of accomplishments of the deceased.

## PART 8: THE SABAZIOS HAND

One of the most enigmatic objects in ancient magic — the hand of Sabazios. More is unknown than known.

**What can be surmised:**

- Sabazios was originally a Median horse god → became a Thracian horse god → became a conflation of the mystical natures of Zeus and Dionysos
- The term "Sabazios" was also used for **non-Jews who adopted Jewish customs**
- The hand is the most potent symbol in magic
- Fingers are always in a **secret cult sign** later taken over as the Christian benediction
- Symbols on the hand include: snake (life and death), pine cone (Dionysos/Demeter = fertility), cow protome (Isis/Io), cup, curule chair with loaves of bread, infant in swaddling, Zeus as god of noon

## PART 9: CURSE TABLETS (DEFIXIONES)

A _defixio_ (plural: _defixiones_) is a curse inscribed on a thin sheet of **lead**, rolled or folded, often pierced with a nail, and deposited in a significant location: buried in a grave (for potency from proximity to the dead), thrown into a river or well, or nailed to a wall.

### Why Lead?

Lead was associated with death and the underworld. Curse tablets are most often lead. The act of depositing them (burial, throwing into water) made them irretrievable and therefore **irreversible**.

### Standard Formula for a Curse Tablet

1. **Invocation**: Deities of the underworld, death, or revenge (Hermes, Persephone, Hekate, Cerberus, Hermes Chthonios, Artemis Chthonia)
2. **Name of target**: including, when possible, the mother's name (more potent than the father's)
3. **How one wishes the person to suffer**: specific, detailed, often physical
4. **Timeline**: when the curse is to take effect
5. **What one will give in return**: the bargain with the deity (food, animal sacrifice, the body of the deceased to torture in Hades)

### Key Examples

**Kamarina (Sicily) curse tablet**: Dionysios, son of Philinos, curses a rival at the **new moon** (more potent); invokes "very powerful snakes" and "giant monkeys" as agents of the curse.

**Baltimore JHU Archaeology Museum (mid-I BC, 5 tablets + nail)**: Curse against Plotius, slave of Avila; invokes Hermes, Proserpina, and Cerberus; wishes Plotius to die of fever from malaria in February (the month of fevers); payment to Cerberus = dates, figs, and one black pig per head; payment to Persephone = the body of the deceased to torture in Hades.

**Piraeus burial curse** (Baltimore JHU, ca. IV BC, 4 tablets + nail): Discovered in the cremation burial of a young girl; four small lead tablets folded in half with a nail; **special potency from being mixed with blood and ashes**; a business curse against husbands and wives of four rival taverns in Piraeus. One translation: _"Hekate Chthonia, Artemis Chthonia, Hermes Chthonios cast your hate upon Phanagora and Demetrios, and their tavern and their property and their possessions. I will bind my enemy Demetrios, and Phanagora, in blood and in ashes, with all the dead..."_

**Bath (England) curse tablet (AD II)**: Curse for the theft of a toga from the baths at Bath — curses could be written for very **small, personal matters**.

**Kerameikos, Athens (IV BC)**: 30 tablets found in a well; against Glykera, a newlywed who had continued with other lovers; after the laws of 388 BC restricted the size of funerals, it became harder to hide curse tablets in sarcophagi, so wells became a common deposit.

**Taranto, Museo Nazionale 109295 (end IV BC)**: Buried in the Necropolis of Metaponto with the daughter of a pharmacy owner; 18 lines cursing the rival pharmacy owner to fall into bankruptcy; more potent because buried in a grave (irretrievable, could not be reversed); also curses 14+ named physicians who used the rival pharmacy: _"First, I bind the workplace of these people and I bind it so it does not operate but remains inactive and suffers misfortune..."_

**Rome, Baths of Diocletian — curse against Judge Sura (end AD IV)**: Magical images include a rhombus (= vagina), image inside rhombus (= fetus inside womb), eyes, cobras, and lactating breasts; Coptic inscription says "eyes"; shows a useless tongue; prayer addressed to nymphs requesting that Judge Sura be blinded, accused of being born from a diseased vulva, and requesting that he never judge any case again.

## PART 10: WITCHCRAFT AND NECROMANCY

### The Concept of "Witch" in Antiquity

The word "witch" is very much a **post-antique construct** — even the Middle Ages were uncertain about what exactly constituted a witch. The ancients were more concerned with **demi-gods and demi-goddesses** who had power to destroy using magic or through the evil eye. Mosaics with "witch" imagery are often **apotropaic** and placed in liminal spots (doors, entries) or danger areas (e.g., slippery floors of baths).

### Historical Context — Early Modern Witchcraft

- **Scots' Book of Witches** (compiled 1658–1662, Wellcome Trust, London): 350 pages; ca. 4,000 witches (very few warlocks); ca. 2,000 killed; based on the 1563 Scots' Witchcraft Act (repealed 1702)
- **Matthew Hopkins** ("Witchfinder General", active 1644–1645): son of a Puritan minister; self-appointed; conducted 250+ trials; up to 19 people burned at the stake at once; his standard technique: strip a woman naked and look for the "mark of the devil"; the threat alone of appearing naked in court was generally enough to obtain a confession
- **Key point:** In antiquity (as later), much of witchcraft prosecution was about **violence against women**

### Famous Ancient Witches and Enchantresses

**Medusa**

- SUPERPOWER: Seeing her turns one to stone; she can **alter the appearance of humans**
- Beheaded by Perseus with help from Athena; the head became a standard decoration on the earliest Greek temples (Temple of Artemis, Corfu, ca. 525 BC)
- Used as a **shield boss** to scare enemies; on coins to deter counterfeiters
- A Medusa medallion on the floor mosaic of a bath (Naples, ca. AD 200) was placed on top of underfloor heating — a particularly dangerous liminal spot

**Circe**

- SUPERPOWER: **Magic potions** that turn men into animals
- Story: Odysseus and crew land on her island; crew drink drugged wine and are turned into animals; Odysseus is protected by a herb given to him by Hermes; he forces Circe to reverse the transformation; one crew member (Eumaeus) **prefers to stay a pig**; Odysseus stays a year and has a son by Circe (Telegonus, who later accidentally kills his father)

**Calypso**

- SUPERPOWER: Her **song** can hold men in her thrall
- She sings to Odysseus while weaving; the deal: 10 years of her singing = immortality, but he must remain with her as her divine consort; Odysseus bails after 7 years

**Medea**

- SUPERPOWER: **Drugs and spells** that draw power from the dead
- Helped Jason obtain the Golden Fleece; when he abandoned her, took revenge
- Magic: drugged the dragon; reconstituted an old ram into a young lamb using herbs; created a poisoned golden robe; summoned a chariot of flying snakes
- Murders: chopped up her brother to slow Jason's escape; tricked Pelias's daughters into killing him; killed Creusa, Creon, and her own two sons by Jason in his presence
- Source: Euripides (tragedy), Pindar, Apollonius Rhodius, Seneca, Ovid (Metamorphoses Book 7)

**Other Named Witches in Ancient Literature**

- Simaetha (Theocritus, Idyll 2)
- Canidia, Folia, Veia, Sagana (Horace, Epodes and Satires)
- Oenothea (Petronius, Satyricon)
- Erichtho (Apuleius, Metamorphoses)
- **Livia**, wife of Augustus, kept a **poisoner on her household staff**

### Transformation as Magic Power

Women who are wronged (especially if their shape is changed) or who commit unspeakable crimes can acquire magical powers:

- **Myrrha/Smyrna**: Tricked her father into sex; transformed into a myrrh tree; as a tree gave birth to **Adonis** (lover of Aphrodite)
- **Daphne**: Pursued by Apollo; begged to be killed rather than raped; transformed into a laurel tree; Apollo made the laurel his tree and founded Delphi near it; laurel leaves chewed by poets for inspiration (= her magical power)
- **Althaea**: Mother of Meleager; possessed a magic log tied to her son's immortality; in a moment of rage (he killed her brothers), she burned it

### Necromancy — Appealing to the Dead

- In Aeschylus's _Persians_, Atossa makes offerings to summon the ghost of her deceased husband Darius
- **Libation tubes**: Long tubes inserted into grave mounds or tile tombs to allow liquid offerings (food and drink) to reach the dead (evidence from Pergamon, Merida in Spain, and Vagnari in Italy)

## PART 11: SYMPATHETIC AND CONTACT MAGIC

### Sympathetic Magic

Sympathetic magic works on the principle that **like affects like** — images, symbols, or materials associated with a desired outcome can bring that outcome about.

- **Breasts on Pylos frescoes**: Breasts as a symbol of survival through childbirth and successful breastfeeding — sympathetic magic for continued success (note: for ancient Mediterranean cultures, bare breasts were not sexualized but represented maternal achievement and survival)
- **Potnia Theron baskanion** (Athens Agora T175, VII BC): Ceramic plaque with holes for attaching to a wooden wall; the protective "Mistress of Animals" — possibly Artemis, Athena, or an unnamed mortal worshipper; ancient parallels most often express a wish; modern parallels: offerings at Lourdes, Greek tamata

### Contact Magic

Contact magic works on the principle that **touching an object activates the spell written on it**.

**Athens Agora P5164** (ca. 450–400 BC, Well H 6:5): Base of a large mixing bowl (_lekane_); three male names inscribed, each described as _kalos_ ("handsome"); _THEOI!_ ("Gods!") beside two of them; invokes gods to make the three named males fall in love with unnamed men; checkerboard pattern is frequent in magic spell graffiti; assumption: when the named males handled the bowl, the magic was activated.

### Deception and Inversion

- **Upside-down vases** (Athens Agora P13094): Depicting a vase on a vase upside-down confuses the evil eye (which is "none too bright")
- **Ambiguous images** (Athens Agora P12178): One horse or two? Collapsing two horses into a single plane confuses the evil eye
- **Dedication to Demeter** (Corinth C-64-1): The prize olpe inscribed _DEWO_ (Demeter) — a dedication to show humility and avoid the attention of Nemesis (who attacks people with too much good fortune)
- **Horror vacui**: The Greek aesthetic principle of filling every empty space with decoration. The evil eye was thought to prefer dark, empty spaces and to gravitate to corners. Note: the Nazis' chief propagandist (Goebbels) had a PhD in Classics from Heidelberg — the swastika in its Greek context was merely a space-filling geometric device.

### Gibberish Inscriptions — Multiple Explanations

Nonsense inscriptions on Greek vases have several possible origins:

1. **Deeply careless or illiterate artist** (many painted parts of vases done by apprentices; misspellings notorious)
2. **Made for Etruscans** — ancient Athenian and Corinthian potters believed that Etruscans thought any inscription added value, so they wrote nonsense
3. **Whimsical** — the inscription can be onomatopoeic (e.g., a hopping rabbit: "hip hip hip hip hip" in Greek)
4. **Magical** — PEOS repeated could be _epoiesen_ (made by) but repetition is characteristic of magic spells; LUSO repeated could be the imperative of _luo_ (destroy) but repetition is characteristic of magic spells; the image of **Herakles and the Nemean lion** was a frequent apotropaic device and appeared in magic spells

## SUPPLEMENTARY CONTEXT

### The Magic of Material

The **material** an object is made from can be its magic — independent of what is written on it or depicted:

- **Ivory** missio token (British Museum 1889.05.20.5): A retiring gladiator normally received a **wooden sword** as his token of freedom; this one is ivory, which has more magical power than wood; it would also have been legal proof of freedom and citizenship for a slave-gladiator
- **Lead** in curse tablets: always associated with death, the underworld, and irreversibility
- **Gold** lamellae: associated with divine promises and positive wishes
- **Bone** in lamellae and dice: bone has more magic than most materials; human bone is more potent; bone of a killed enemy is most potent of all
- **Alloys** (copper-gold): require more work and are therefore more potent than pure metals

### Syncretism and Ancient Magic

Ancient magic was profoundly **syncretic** — it mixed deities, symbols, and formulas from multiple traditions freely:

- The Nea Paphos amulet mixes mystical Judaism (_IAEW_ = Yahweh) with Egyptian solar cult (Re) and Orphic symbolism
- The Billingford lamella invokes Iao (Yahweh) and Abrasax (Gnostic deity) in Greek, written in Latin
- Babylonian demon bowls use pseudo-scripts from multiple religious communities
- The Sabazios hand blends Median, Thracian, Dionysiac, and Orphic symbolism
