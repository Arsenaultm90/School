
# Animals as Technology

> Animals are not just food — they are **resources**, **tools**, **commodities**, and **symbols**

Animal husbandry includes:
- Food production (honey, fish)
- Processing and preservation (salt, garum)
- Trade and transport
- Religion and ritual
- Entertainment and spectacle
- Political power and prestige



---
# Apiculture (Beekeeping) – High-Value Section

#### Specialized Technology
- Bee smokers:
    - Wide mouth
    - Blow hole to force smoke deeper
- Specialized extraction tools:
    - Long wooden handles
    - Scraping and cutting tools

> Presence of specialized tools = skilled craft + investment

#### Honey Extraction & Storage
**Honey Strainer**
- Comb placed on raised knobs
- Honey drips through hole into amphora

**Storage Amphorae**
- Early example: **7th century BC**
- Bee painted on rim → clear labeling

**Ant-Trap Honey Pots**
- Raised lip filled with water or oil
- Ants drown before reaching honey
- Proper storage:
    - Cool
    - Dry
    - Protected from smoke
- Honey can last **up to 10 years**


#### Large Storage Jars (Vetena)
- Byzantine period
- ~1.5 m high
- Spigot at bottom:
    - Reduces waste
    - Reduces contact with human skin
- Reason:
    - Human skin enzymes harden honey
- Reuse:
    - After honey → sweet wine

**Greek proverb**
> “Take honey from the top, wine from the middle, oil from the bottom”


#### Bee Hives
**Vertical Ceramic Hives**
- Built into hillsides
- Covered with straw and potsherds
- Cooling + protection
- Bees identify hive by pheromones

**Transportable Hives**
- Moved by donkey
- Purpose:
    - Exploit different blossoms
    - Produce different honey flavours
- Indicates awareness of:
    - Thyme honey
    - Citrus honey
    - Floral variation        

**Domestic Wall Hives**
- Built into house walls
- Benefits:
    - Protection from predators
    - Protection from wet/cold
    - Easy human access from inside


#### Wax Processing
- Wax press:
    - Known from Classical Greek period
    - Uses **Archimedean screw**
- Similar to wine press technology
- Small size → less mechanical stress
- Wax left after final honey extraction

#### Archaeological Implication
> Beekeeping sites are easy to identify due to specialized equipment

# Fishing – Technology and Diet

#### Contribution to Diet
- Degree of importance debated
- Requires:
    - Skill
    - Specialized equipment

#### Fishing Methods (Increasing Efficiency)
1. **Pole and Hook**
    - Hardest
    - Least productive
    - Most “boring”
2. **Spear Fishing**
    - More active
    - Requires visibility and strength
3. **Net Fishing**
    - Most efficient
    - Group labor
4. **Hand Fishing**
    - Risky
    - Used for octopus


#### Artistic Evidence
- Dedications of fish (especially coastal areas)
- Humorous mosaics:
    - Bored fishermen
    - Sneering fish
    - Erotic parody
- Species identifiable → realism


#### Octopus Fishing
- Extremely territorial
- Lured with jars
- Dangerous:
    - Can bite
- Preparation:
    - Must be beaten on rock to tenderize

# Fish Processing & Preservation

#### Nets
- Lead net weights
- Found near harbors and ship sheds

#### Fish Tanks (Piscinae)
- Used to hold **live fish**
- Roman period
- Short-distance transport only
- Passive water exchange via grills
- Maintenance stairs included

#### Fish Processing Areas
- Located at water’s edge
- Process:
    - Remove head, tail, guts
    - Flesh salted
    - Offal → **garum** (fish sauce)
- Rock features:
    - Table supports
    - Gut-catching depressions

# Salt – Essential Technology

#### Importance
> Salt was the **main preservative** in antiquity

#### Salt Pans
- Passive economy:
    - High initial labor
    - Very low ongoing labor

- Process:
    - Winter storms fill pans
    - Summer evaporation
    - Fall harvest
    - Winter rain washes salt

#### Control
- Medieval Orthodox Church:
    - Salt monopoly

- Modern Greece:
    - Still a state monopoly

# Animal Entertainment (Very Exam-Likely)

#### Venationes (Animal Hunts)
- More common than gladiatorial combat

- Animals:
    - Bears
    - Boars
    - Lions

- Occurred in:
    - Amphitheaters
    - Theatres
    - Circuses

#### Animal Importers
- Specialized profession
- Captured animals via nets
- Supplied games and spectacles

#### Combat and Consumption
- Animals forced to fight
    
- Afterwards:
    - Animals usually consumed

- Occasionally animal “wins”
    - Rare, notable

#### Decline of Spectacle
- Animal sacrifice and gladiatorial games declined:
    - In tandem with Christianity
    - But **not caused solely by it**

- Christianity accelerated an existing trend
