## PART 2: THE LYRE

The lyre is the most widely documented musical instrument in the ancient world, appearing across millennia and cultures.

### Earliest Examples

- **Ur, Royal Cemetery** (British Museum inv. 121199, ca. 2600 BC): silver, ornamental; 11 strings; bull head at end of sounding board; lapis lazuli and red limestone inlay with scenes of gazelles, lions, and goats; modern reconstructions for uprights and bridge
- **Tomb of Ani, Egypt** (British Museum EA 24564, ca. 1500 BC – pre-1104 BC): ladle-shaped; 5 strings; upper part in cattle bone; pegs in hippo ivory; top of sounding board in reed; underside has figure wearing both the white and red crowns of Egypt

### Greek and Etruscan Lyres

- **Herakleion, VIII BC**: 4-string bronze lyre player; sense of being lost in the music
- **Samos, VII BC**: ivory arm from a lyre (wood lost); dedication to Hera at her sanctuary
- **Rome, Villa Giulia (ca. 525–510 BC)**: Lysippides Painter — Herakles plays a **7-string lyre** for Athena (in full armour) and Hermes (who listens appreciatively); from Tarquinia
- **Rome, Villa Giulia, inv. 50627 (ca. 520–510 BC)**: trefoil oinochoe — Orpheus with foot on steps; graffito of merchant "H I" on underside; inscription KALOS ORPHEUS painted on the vase (meaning "Orpheus rocks," not literally "Orpheus is handsome")
- **Sparta, V BC**: Apollo holds lyre in left hand, small cup in right; Artemis pours wine; two birds flank an omphalos → scene takes place at Delphi
- **British Museum E308 (ca. 460–450 BC)**: attributed to Zannoni Painter — male dressed as a woman (snood, chiton, peplos) plays a 7-string lyre; possibly a male actor in a female chorus role
- **Athens, Agora Museum (ca. 450 BC)**: young man practices lyre; naked; holds plectrum (pick); expression of intense concentration
- **British Museum 1836.0224.114 (ca. 450 BC)**: fully mature male tilts his head back and sings to himself after hunting; the words painted coming from his mouth read "Hipparchos kalos"; 7-string lyre
- **Rome, Villa Giulia kylix (ca. 450 BC)**: lyre held at a rakish angle; names of the player's beloved painted coming from his mouth
- **Schwerin Museum inv. 708 (ca. 470 BC)**: signed by **Pistoxenos** ("trustworthy foreigner"); music lesson — old man Linos teaches Iphikles (brother of Herakles); two different types of lyre shown; reverse has Iphikles walking to his lesson with a _paedagogos_ (tutor)
- **British Museum 1917.0701.171 (ca. 300–250 BC)**: limestone woman with lyre from Larnaka, Cyprus; worshipper or priestess; holds plectrum; 7-string lyre with red paint on strings still faintly visible

### Roman Lyres

- **Ischia Archaeological Museum (reign of Augustus)**: relief of Apollo semi-nude, holds lyre in left hand, griffin looks back at him; inscribed by a spa visitor
- **Rome, Baths of Diocletian (AD I)**: tombstone for **Hymnis Gellia** — died age 12 years 10 months; _Psaltria_ = lyre player; "Hymnis" may be a performance name (from _hymn = hymn singer_)
- **Pompeii, House of Vettii (pre-AD 79)**: fresco of putti from a dining room — two putti work on a lyre as part of a scene of putti tending a garden and crafting objects
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale (pre-AD 79)**: fresco of a centaur giving a music lesson; centaur wears chaplet; student (naked with mantle at neck) stares intently while holding a **9-string lyre**
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale, Dirce Group (½ AD I)**: tortoise shell lyre next to seated musician; scene of Dirce (the "wicked stepmother") who tormented Antiope — Amphion and Zethus eventually tie Dirce to a bull as punishment
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale, inv. 6281 (AD II)**: Apollo seated with lyre in **porphyry** (purple stone reserved for emperor and his family); thought to be a gift from Hadrian to a local temple; originally acquired by the Farnese family in the 16th century with rusting bronze head, hands, and feet replaced in marble
- **Rome, Baths of Diocletian, inv. 214 (AD 160–180)**: medallion sarcophagus found on the Appian Way near the Church of Saints Nero and Achilles; music lessons on **both ends** — centaurs giving lessons to skeptical young male students, both holding plectrums in their right hands

## PART 3: THE AULOS (DOUBLE FLUTE)

The _aulos_ (often mistranslated as "flute") was actually a **double-pipe reed instrument** — two pipes held simultaneously, producing a rich, penetrating sound. It was the primary instrument of Greek drama, religious ritual, and Dionysiac processions.

### Early Examples

- **Berlin, New Kingdom Egypt (ca. 1540–1075 BC)**: two wooden flutes; lower one tied with papyrus
- **British Museum 1816.0601.502 (V/IV BC)**: wooden flutes from Athens; mouthpieces intact; donated by Lord Bruce (who received them from Lord Elgin)
- **Corinth Archaeological Museum inv. 202931**: two **bone flutes** (unpublished); holes irregular; one made in two pieces

### Archaic and Classical Examples

- **Rome, Villa Giulia (IV BC, from Tarquinia)**: single flute, local production; main shaft bone with coating; 18th-century restored mouthpiece
- **Rome, Villa Giulia (ca. 500 BC, Necropolis of Banditaccia)**: attributed to the Teroulos Painter — satyr with **double flute**; man bun; goat skin over shoulder; long scraggly beard; panther pelt tied to right wrist; prominent penis
- **British Museum E270 (ca. 480 BC)**: attributed to Kleophrades Painter — male flute player wearing a **harness on his head to hold the double flute in place**; dressed as a woman; on a platform = taking part in a competition
- **Nafplio Archaeological Museum (ca. 420–400 BC)**: two women at altar — one plays double flute, one plays tambourine; garland suspended from top centre of vase; altar between them

### Roman Flutes

- **Naples, Museo Nazionale (AD II)**: woman with single flute; high-waisted dress; holes equidistant on both sides
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale, inv. 76891 (pre-AD 79, from Boscoreale)**: actual silver and bronze flute with ivory mouthpiece — rare survival of the instrument itself
- **Triumph of Dionysos sarcophagus, Baths of Diocletian (AD II)**: centaur plays flute while pulling Dionysos's chariot; second centaur has lyre; winged Eros opens Ariadne's dress; sleeping Ariadne; Silenos propped up by satyrs
- **Marriage of Dionysos sarcophagus, Baths of Diocletian (AD II)**: lid has seated figure playing flute as dinner music; erotes fly between dining couples; body shows Dionysos and Ariadne at their wedding feast attended by Hermes with caduceus

### The Dionysiac _Thiasos_ (Procession)

Multiple Naples reliefs (AD II) show the Dionysiac _thiasos_ — a procession of fully human-form devotees:

- Silenos plays double flute; wears short comedy cloak with garment weights; boots from comedy
- Woman plays a **complex double flute** without a harness — technically puzzling how the fingering could be managed
- Naked male in ecstatic dance holds thyrsus; drunken woman held up by male companion; tambourine visible

## PART 4: THE SYRINX (PAN PIPES)

The syrinx is a set of pipes of graduated length bound together — the instrument of Pan and the countryside.

- **Naples, Museo Nazionale (late Republic/early Empire)**: bronze syrinx with 11 pipes; decorative shrines and two single flutes illustrated on it
- **Rome, Baths of Diocletian**: tombstone of **Scirtus Cornelianus**, musician (_symphonicus_), with syrinx depicted
- **Pan teaching syrinx to Dafni** (Naples, 2/2 AD II): based on Rhodian original by Heliodorus; Pan has clear sexual interest in the androgynously-named Dafni; Dafni's hair in an Aphrodite knot underscores the erotic undertone; syrinx has **8 pipes = one octave**; dancing erotes; palm of victory at base; Priapus on right edge situates the scene in the countryside
- **Statue of Pan** (Naples, AD II): marble support leg indicating an earlier bronze original; syrinx has 11 pipes (5 at the same highest note); cudgel for rabbit hunting
- **Oxford, Ashmoleon Museum (ca. AD 200)**: object identified as a syrinx is almost certainly a **mold for nails** — 7 slots of equal diameter; small hole for hanging up; clay means the mold could be heated when molten metal was poured in

## PART 5: CYMBALS AND KROTALA

### Cymbals

- **Berlin dancer (Altes Museum inv. SK208, Roman copy of ca. 340 BC)**: cymbals visible in fragments hanging on tree stump
- **Naples floor mosaic (pre-AD 79)**: musicians ran away when a panther appeared — cymbals, a drinking horn (_rhyton_), and a stool with the contortionist's clothing are abandoned; panther is the animal of Dionysos
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale (AD II)**: satyr about to bang cymbals together while infant Dionysos holds grapes just out of reach — replica of a well-known Hellenistic bronze

### Krotala (Castanets/Clappers)

Krotala were wooden or bone clappers. In early black-figure vase painting they were played mainly by **women**; from ca. 500 BC they were increasingly played by **men and satyrs**.

- **Frankfurt, Liebieghaus inv. 1515 (ca. 500–450 BC)**: satyr in ecstasy clicking krotala
- **British Museum E241 (ca. 400–300 BC)**: Adonia festival scene — five women + flying Eros on a rooftop; one woman clicks krotala; one plays aulos; another in rapture; associated with **Aphrodite Genetyllis** on a ladder
    - **Adonia**: a women-only rooftop festival; women spent the day and night away from men
- **Princeton University Museum inv. 33.34 (ca. 425–420 BC)**: woman at altar with krotala outside a house — possibly a free Athenian woman celebrating Adonia; could also be a _hetaira_ (high-end sex worker) — context determines meaning
    - Note: A special dance called _Anakreontic_ involved women dancing and men dressed as women clicking krotala; several Greek rites involved ritual cross-dressing and men transgressing female-only space

## PART 10: ORGANS

### The Hydraulic Organ (_Hydraulis_)

- Invented by **Ctesibius of Alexandria** (ca. 300 BC)
- Powered by **two hydraulic pumps** using a teeter-totter action to maintain consistent air pressure
- **Foot pedals** on two sides
- Archaeological evidence: Dion (northern Greece) — hydraulic organ discovered in rescue excavation; now reconstructed at the Museum of Ancient Greek Technology, Herakleion
- **British Museum token, inv. R4912 (AD 211–217, reign of Caracalla; countermarked AD IV/V)**: shows organ player seated looking out toward the token holder

_(Scholarship: Stefan Hagel (2023), "Inside the Hydra: Taking the Ancient Water Organ Seriously," Greek and Roman Musical Studies 11: 52–81)_

## PART 11: MUSIC THEORY

### [Pseudo-]Plutarch, _On Music_

- Attributed to Plutarch (ca. AD 100) but likely written in AD III/IV
- The **longest essay** in the Plutarchan corpus; _On Isis and Osiris_ is the longest of the genuinely authentic works
- Key arguments:
    - Music is **impossible without words** — no purely instrumental music
    - Music is essential to **oratory**
    - A person who cannot play the lyre **cannot claim to be literate**
    - Music is the key to **religion** (especially oracles) and therefore the underpinning of all philosophy

### Pythagoras (ca. 580–496 BC, from Samos)

- Mathematician and music theorist
- Understood music as a branch of **mathematics** (string ratios produce musical intervals)
- Believed music and mathematics could together **prove the immortality of the soul**
- Developed the theory of **transmigration of souls** (metempsychosis/reincarnation) — the majority of ancient religions believed in some form of reincarnation; Pythagoras made this central to ancient philosophy and religion
- His ideas were crucial to **Plato** and later to Plutarch

## PART 12: MUSICAL NOTATION

### Background

- Surviving Greek musical notation dates only from the **V BC onward**
- Earlier music was tied to travelling bards and was largely **improvised**
- Earliest Roman music was tied to **religious festivals**
- After the Roman conquest of Greece in **146 BC**, Greek musicians flooded Rome
- Music became central to the curriculum of **aristocratic children**; in the Roman Empire, musicians could be extremely well-paid celebrities
- Transcribing music in the modern sense (as Bach did, 1685–1750) is a **very recent development**

### Key Documents

**Louvre G490 (ca. 410 BC)** — Bell-krater attributed to the Pothos Painter:

- Scene of the music contest of **Apollo and Marsyas**; a Muse holds a _kylindros_ (parchment scroll) with written music — proving notation existed by at least the V BC
- Three surviving versions of this scene by the Pothos Painter
- The contest ends in the **flaying alive of Marsyas**

**Epitaph of Seikilos** (Copenhagen, National Museum of Denmark, AD I/II):

- Found at Tralles; columnar tombstone
- Inscription: _"I am an image, not a stele. Seikilos placed me here as a deathless remembrance as an everlasting monument"_
- Contains the **oldest complete surviving song** with both melody and words

**Hymns from the Athenian Treasury, Delphi (128 BC)**:

- Carved on the outer wall of the Athenian Treasury (post-480 BC)
- **Oldest surviving written notation of a melody**
- Notes for both chorus and accompanying instruments — comparable to Leonard Cohen's "Bird on a Wire" with both guitar chords and vocal melody
- Uses combination of Greek **alphabet letters and non-alphabetic symbols**; Α is assumed the lowest note, Θ an octave higher
- Hymns composed by **Athenaios and Limenion**; sung by a large Athenian choir in procession for the opening of the Pythian Games of Apollo; accompanied by lyre and flute

**Vienna papyrus G 2315 (play dated 408 BC; papyrus AD III)**:

- Contains lines 338–344 of **Euripides' _Orestes_**
- Musical papyri are presumed to be **practice scripts for choruses** — this proves the play was still being performed 800+ years after it was written
- Notes above the text represent chording

**Oslo University Museum POslo 1413 (AD I/II)**:

- Part of what appears to have been an **anthology of 12 songs** from drama and epic
- Contains a song about the apotheosis of the dead Achilles as a minor god
- Lines divided into phrases for easier memorization
- Wide margins for performer's personal annotations
- **Different letterforms suggest multiple owners or performers** used the sheet music
- Wide spaces between words for _melisma_ (singing multiple notes on one syllable)

**Yale Classical Papyrus inv. 4510**:

- Two songs with **different notations for voice (alphabet letters) and for instruments (symbols)**

## PART 13: DANCE

### General Context

Key ancient sources: Lucian, _On Dance_ and Athenaeus, _Deipnosophistae_ (discusses dance in multiple places).

### Early Dance Images

**Athens, National Museum NAM 784 (ca. 710–700 BC)**:

- Late Geometric scyphos from the Dipylon Grave
- 4 women holding hands dancing in front of a wedding bed; a male and a (rare) female centaur are also present

**Washington, DC, Smithsonian inv. 136380 (ca. 520–510 BC)**:

- Signed by **Epictetos**; inscribed "Hipparchus kalos"
- Figure dances to the right while looking left; holds a wine cup and an oinochoe; wearing a festive chaplet; himation draped over shoulders

**Boston MFA 65.908 (ca. 450 BC)**:

- White-ground phiale; 7 women dancing around a blazing altar; flute player; _kophos_ basket with scarves

### Dionysiac Dance — Satyrs and Maenads

Much of our evidence for ancient dancing comes from depictions of **satyrs** (male half-horse companions of Dionysos) and **maenads** (female ecstatic devotees). These figures appear on pottery from at least the VII BC onward and represent both actual cultic dancing and its idealized mythological version.

**Key dance moves documented on vases:**

- Touching head and bottom simultaneously (Munich, Antikensammlungen inv. 1431, ca. 600–575 BC)
- **Bum bump** — sticking bottoms out and bumping (Stanford University inv. 1961.66, ca. 600–575 BC)
- Slapping the bottom of one's own foot (Louvre, ca. 480 BC)
- Extended arm slapping women's bottoms — described as an Etruscan dance move (British Museum B 41)

**Graffiti and inscriptions on dancing vases:**

- Vases with dancers often have nonsense names or fun nicknames scratched as _graffiti_, either by potters in the last firing or by subsequent owners
- Some names appear to describe dance moves or comment humorously on the dancers' posteriors
- Inscriptions at waist level or lower on Tyrrhenian (Etruscan-market) vases indicate erotic appeal

**Rome, Villa Giulia, Nikosthenic amphora (ca. 525–520 BC)**: shoulder signed by Nikosthenes; women clothed, men naked; meant for the Etruscan market

**London, British Museum inv. B41** (Tydeus Painter, from Corinth): clothed males, naked females; graffiti with nonsense names of faux dances; several male arms extended to slap bottoms of females

### The Pyrrhic Dance

The Pyrrhic was a **weapons dance** — performed in full or partial armour with shield and spear. It was:

- A **sport at the ancient Olympic Games**
- Performed by both men and women
- Associated with Athena and Artemis

**Evidence:**

- **Berlin, Staatliche Museum inv. 1966.19 (ca. 510 BC)**: signed by Smikros — satyr performs Pyrrhic dance with cavalry shield; unusually, the shield is shown from the inside
- **Louvre inv. Cp 10846 (ca. 460 BC)**: attributed to Niobid Painter — female Pyrrhic dancer with shield, spear, and shorts, following a flute player in procession
- **Naples, Museo Nazionale inv. 81908 (ca. 440 BC)**: female Pyrrhic warrior dances before Artemis's altar (Side A); cult statue of Artemis on Side B

### Cross-Dressing in Dance and Festival

Several Greek festivals explicitly involved **men dressing as women**, getting drunk, and dancing:

- At dinner parties after the two great festivals of Dionysos, men cross-dressed by tradition
- **Festival of Hercules the Cross-Dresser**: men dressed as women, got drunk, and danced
- A dance called _Anakreontic_ involved women dancing and men dressed as women clicking krotala
- **Malibu, Getty Museum inv. 86.AE.296 (ca. 460 BC)**: elderly komasts in Persian dress; graffito on bottom dedicating the cup to Aphrodite
- **Madrid, Museo Arqueológico Nacional inv. 11009 (ca. 500–480 BC)**: men dressed as women with parasols — parasols were associated with women at the **Thesmophoria** (major festival of Demeter); could be men parodying that festival

### Kalathiskos Dance

A vigorous, often acrobatic dance involving **kalathos baskets** (weaving baskets used as props):

- Usually performed by pre-marital age women
- Generally naked; vigorous movement; acrobatic moves practiced
- **Piraeus, Archaeological Museum inv. 20542 (last quarter V BC)**: possibly depicts the brothers of Helen watching her birth from an egg; kalathos prominently featured — indicates _kalathiskos_ dance

### Wedding Dance

- **Athens, Moutoussi Collection (ca. 450 BC)**: red-figure wedding pyxis (_pyxis_ was often a wedding present from groom to bride) — shows bride and friends preparing for the wedding: lyre player, women practicing dance, woman adorning herself in a mirror, woman at bath

## PART 14: POPULAR PERFORMANCE

### Busking and Street Performance

**Naples, Museo Nazionale inv. H3232 (ca. 475–425 BC)**: signed by Polygnotos — a remarkable depiction of performers _practicing_:

- Two women dance the **kalathiskos** (basket dance)
- Flute player provides music
- One woman practices a **sword dance (Pyrrhic)** — generally done by males; she wears "satyr shorts"
- A **contortionist** practices bending over backwards to drink wine from a kylix
- Woman with krotala beats time for the Pyrrhic dancer

**Berlin, Altes Museum inv. F3444 (ca. 350–300 BC)**: female acrobat shoots arrows from her feet while bent over backwards; wears satyr shorts

### Symposia and Satyr Entertainment

The Symposium (drinking party) was a key venue for music, dance, and bawdy performance.

**Brussels trick vase (Musées Royaux d'Art et d'Histoire, inv. R248, ca. 575–560 BC)**:

- Neck amphora showing satyrs and maenads dancing; nonsense inscriptions in Corinthian alphabet
- X-ray reveals it is a **trick vase** (_thauma_): hollow interior reduced to only 1 _kotyle_ (8 ounces) of actual volume; 4 holes in the bottom to prevent explosion in firing
- The host would fill it and drain it, to the astonishment of guests who expected he was drinking several liters of wine

**Baltimore, Johns Hopkins Museum inv. 1781 (ca. 460 BC)**:

- Interior: satyr with drinking horn, wine skin, and vine; several chaplets fall in a dildo-shaped cluster
- Exterior: cavorting satyrs; deeply uninterested woman

**Munich, Staatliche Antikensammlungen inv. 2647 (last quarter V BC)**:

- Side A: satyr plays double pipe; other satyrs try to undress a maenad and a man wearing women's clothing
- Side B: three satyrs and three women in ecstatic dance

**Berlin, Altes Museum inv. SK195 (Roman marble copy of Hellenistic bronze)**:

- Satyr and hermaphrodite; neither head original to statue (satyr head is ancient but from a different statue; hermaphrodite head is a Renaissance addition)
- Hermaphrodite wears upper-arm torque of a courtesan
- Props include pipe, cymbals, a cudgel for rabbit hunting, and a **turtle** — sign of long life / devotion over a lifetime

### Plays and Theatre

Ancient theatre was inseparable from music — all dramatic performances were accompanied by instruments, and the chorus both sang and danced.

**Actor as Maenad (Berlin, Altes Museum inv. V.1.3223, ca. 470–460 BC)**: actor holds lower leg of a goat and a sword; piper plays double aulos with head strap; depicts a **chorus of a play**

**Psykter of hoplites on dolphins (NY Met inv. 1989.281.69, ca. 520–510 BC)**: attributed to Oltos — Greek hoplites with helmets, shields, and spears riding dolphins; presumed to be the **chorus of a Greek play** (like the horse costumes in Aristophanes' _Knights_ or the bird costumes in _Birds_)

- The relationship between vases and plays is always tricky — the vase often **predates** the play it is thought to illustrate

**Actor as Argos (Athens, Agora Museum, ca. 450 BC)**: actor in costume of Argus (eyes on costume) holds double flute; second actor wears _skinnis_ (satyr leather shorts with erect penis) and holds mask of Silenos — refers to a **satyr play** in which satyrs comically "helped" (and failed to help) free Io, Zeus's pregnant mistress, from the many-eyed Argus

**Satyr play actors (NY Met inv. 25.78.66, ca. 420 BC)**: attributed to Polion — three lyre players in horse-tail and horse-ear costumes; double aulos player faces them, looking desperately bored; middle lyre player shown with **infibulation** (penis tied up — worn by professional performers to prevent erection during energetic performance); blocking or rehearsal in progress

**Satyr chorus mosaic (Naples, Museo Nazionale, pre-AD 79, from Pompeii)**: 7 actors of different ages dressing and preparing for a satyr play:

- Two actors on left in satyr shorts
- Actor further right with mask on his head
- Central figure plays double flute
- Seated older actor in the costume of a **wandering philosopher** with walking stick
- Three theatre masks visible, including one of Silenos (oldest of the satyrs)
- Stagehand attends to a costume

**Masked chorus from Olbia (Kiev, National Academy of Sciences inv. AM 1097/5219, ca. 420 BC)**:

- Flute player and tambourine player inscribed _kalos_ ("handsome / well done")
- Two male actors in female costume with white-painted masks inscribed _kale_ ("beautiful / well done")
- Circles on garments = sequins or decorative buttons
- Proves that male choruses in female costume were being performed and celebrated

### Funerals

Music and dance were integral to ancient funerary ritual — not as entertainment but as a means of helping the deceased pass into the afterlife.

**Berlin Siren Stele (Altes Museum inv. SK755, Athens, ca. 400 BC)**:

- Sirens carved on top; servant helps deceased choose jewelry for the next world
- **Sirens**, after being defeated by Odysseus, became accompaniers of the deceased (particularly women) to the underworld; their song aided the soul's entry into the afterlife

**Rome, Museo Barracco (beginning V BC, from Chiusi)**:

- Four-sided tombstone (_cippus_)
- Music and dance at the _prothesis_ (laying out of the body, left panel) and the **funeral banquet** — the deceased takes part

**Triumph of Dionysos sarcophagus, Baths of Diocletian (AD II)**:

- Dionysiac sarcophagi invariably include music and dancing
- End panel: satyr disrobing a maenad

**Copenhagen, Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek inv. 854 (AD III)**:

- Strigil sarcophagus; woman playing a stringed instrument flanked by comic and tragic masks

**Copenhagen, Ny Carlsberg Glyptotek inv. 853 (AD IV)**:

- Sarcophagus of Comedy and Tragedy
- Figures on ends hold comic and tragic masks; woman in centre holds an unidentified object

## SUPPLEMENTARY CONTEXT

### Music and Social Status in Rome

- After 146 BC (Roman conquest of Greece), **Greek musicians flooded Rome**
- Music became a central part of the curriculum for **children of aristocratic families** — playing the lyre was considered as fundamental as reading and writing
- Professional musicians in the Roman Empire could be **extremely well-paid celebrities**
- [Pseudo-]Plutarch states explicitly: _"A person who cannot play the lyre cannot claim to be able to read and write"_

### The Adonia Festival

A women-only festival in honour of Adonis, the beautiful young man beloved by Aphrodite and contested by Persephone:

- **Myth**: Adonis born of incest (Smyrna/Myrrha tricked her father); Persephone refuses to return him to Aphrodite; Zeus intervenes — 4 months with Aphrodite, 4 with Persephone, 4 months of Adonis's own choice (he chooses Aphrodite); alternate version: Adonis killed by a boar
- **Festival rite**: women spent the day and night on rooftops, away from men; music and ecstatic dancing
- _Parallel_: Dumuzi/Tammuz and Inanna/Ishtar (Mesopotamian equivalent myth)
- Krotala, aulos, and _tympana_ (tambourines) were the characteristic instruments of the Adonia

### Infibulation of Performers

Professional Greek and Roman performers wore **infibulation** — a tie or clasp restraining the foreskin — to prevent erection during physically demanding performance. This is visible on the NY Met satyr play krater and is also mentioned in literary sources. It was a practical performance convention, not a punishment.

### The Relationship Between Vases and Plays

A recurring methodological caution in this lecture: vases depicting what looks like theatrical scenes often **predate** the plays they are assumed to illustrate. This means either:

1. The vase depicts an **earlier version** of the myth performed as a play we no longer have, or
2. The vase depicts a **generic mythological scene** that was later given theatrical form The relationship is always complex and should not be assumed to be straightforward illustration.
