## PART 1: ETRUSCAN BURIAL PRACTICES

### Villanovan Culture (ca. 900 – ca. 700 BC)
- Early Iron Age culture of northern Italy; superseded by Etruscans
- Primary burial practice: **cremation**
- Cremated ashes stored in a medium-size ceramic storage vessel (often misnamed "hut urn")
- Lid could be shaped like a ceramic human head, or an actual bronze helmet of the period
- General rule: **the simpler the lid, the earlier the date**

### Cinerary Urns — Form & Meaning
- **Hut Urns** (Vulci, mid-VII BC): lids shaped like roofs with smoke holes, rafters, and rope-tie details; wide door for inserting ashes
    - Bronze and terracotta versions exist
    - Greek comparison (Argos, VIII/VII BC): temple-style hut urn with columns in antis
- **Temple Urns** (Vulci, III–I BC): ceramic roof tiles, acroteria, anthemia (floral motifs), 6 columns — wide opening at base for ashes
- **House Urns** (Rome, Villa Giulia, late Etruscan): gable roof with swan acroteria (swan = immortality), palmette feet (palmette = immortality), breasts along bottom plank
- **Stoa Urn** (Rome, Villa Giulia, late Etruscan): shaped like a strip-mall colonnade; columns on plinths, triple beam across front

### Tholos Tombs (Regional Variant to Tumulus)
- **Casale Marimittimo** (ca. 620 BC): corbelled roof, limestone walls, central pilaster for support
- **Mula** (ca. 630–600 BC): corbelled roof, benches along walls to place ash urns — more efficient for later communal use
- **Volterra, Room of Urns** (II BC): rock-cut, central support post, benches for urns; urns styled like kline sarcophagi with carved lids showing deceased feasting

### Tarquinia Tomb Paintings
All tombs replicate an **Etruscan dining room** interior. Key features: ceiling lines for roof beams, pediment decoration, bands of floral motifs.

|Tomb|Date|Key Imagery|
|---|---|---|
|**Tomb of the Leopards**|ca. 480–470 BC|Two leopards; men & women on couches in animated conversation; naked servants|
|**Tomb of the Bulls** (Tomba dei Tori)|VI BC|Bull ignores heterosexual couple; bull charges homosexual couple; Achilles ambushing Troilus; two door-shaped chambers = passage to underworld|
|**Tomb of the Olympics** (Tomba delle Olimpiadi)|ca. 520–500 BC|Boxers at entry to burial chamber; steps down from tumulus|
|**Tomb of the Augurs** (Tomba degli Auguri)|ca. 540–520 BC|Two augurs (diviners) face painted door to underworld; bird of omen visible|
|**Tomb of the Lionesses** (Tomba delle Leonesse)|ca. 525–500 BC|Two lionesses with multiple breasts; exuberant dancing; lyre & double flute players; large mixing krater; dolphins in lower band|

> **Note:** Etruscans often favoured scenes of Greeks behaving _unheroically_ (e.g., Achilles as trickster, not hero).

### Other Etruscan Burial Objects

- **Cremation kit, Villa Giulia** (ca. 530 BC): red impasto casserole with ashes + last dinner instruments — bucchero kantharoi, Attic black-figure kylix, brochette sticks, ash-sorting stick, **eggs** (symbol of achieving afterlife)
- **Clay Sarcophagi** (Villa Giulia): fired in multiple pieces; couples posed as if at a dinner party — wife in animated conversation, husband's arm around wife
- **Monte Abatone cemetery** (2/2 VI BC): woman on dining couch, pouring perfume from alabastron, semi-nude with cape, shell necklace
- **Altar of Vel Epzus** (found near Vulci, III BC): four-sided limestone altar, Etruscan inscription, evidence of incense burning; figure of Vel shown larger than the sacellum = sign of deification after death; side panel shows Etruscan door to the underworld

## PART 2: ROMAN BURIAL PRACTICES

### Imperial Mausolea
- **Mausoleum of Augustus** (begun ca. 10 BC): Etruscan-style tumulus; first emperors buried here (Augustus, Tiberius, Claudius, Vespasian, Titus); Julii and Claudii claimed descent from Etruscans; Cyprus trees planted = trees of death; tombstone of Agrippina (mother of Nero) survives with inscription identifying "the bones of Agrippina"
- **Mausoleum of Hadrian**: emperors from Hadrian (d. AD 128) onward buried here; successor to Mausoleum of Augustus once it filled; today the Popes are taken here for their final illness

### Cremation Vessels & Markers
- **Columbarium** (Ostia, early II AD): "dovecote" structure for cremation urns in niches; covered slabs + inscribed stelai; above-ground equivalent of catacombs; Roman burial split between cremation and inhumation until victory of Christianity
- **Ash urn in shape of wicker basket** (Rome, Baths of Diocletian, AD I): burnt bones still inside; wicker weave, clip fastener, and dowel frame all indicated in stone
- **Cinerary urn lid** (Baths of Diocletian): inscription reads VICARIOS = a slave owned by a slave (in Roman law, if one parent is free, child is free)
- **Urn lid with Lar and son** (Baths of Diocletian): iron handle with lead seal; dedication to the household gods (DM = to the shades)
- **Titulus grave marker** (Baths of Diocletian): house-number style inscription; reverse has guidelines for practice inscriptions; format later adopted in catacombs

### Pompeii — Plaster Casts of Victims
- Plaster fills the cavity where a vaporized corpse once was, preserving posture and clothing
- Baths of Stabiae: hand shielding face
- Macellum (fish market): hand shielding face; one person gave up and sat down waiting to die
- _Photo credits: Wick Dosset_

### Portable Altar (Baths of Diocletian, I AD)
- Ceramic, hollow; evidence of burning = used outdoors (outside town)
- Scene: Silenus pushes donkey → donkey unimpressed → winged figure (Kairos? = chance of immortality) on donkey → satyr leads
- Possibly a parody of "Triumph of Dionysos" or "Return of Hephaistos to Olympus"

## PART 3: SARCOPHAGI

### Kline (Couch) Sarcophagi
The deceased is shown reclining on a **dining couch** as if at a banquet — symbolizing the afterlife as eternal feasting.

- **inv. 2002870** (ca. AD 300–310, Luna marble): deceased holds bust of female family member; hole drilled in couch back for ongoing dedications by family
- **inv. 114906** (kline lid of adolescent): no bulla = over 14; pomegranate in hand = sign of achieving afterlife; two pillows
- **inv. 39504** (end AD I, Via Portuense): young female in chiton and peplos; buttons along arms; servant holds basket of fruit; tasseled pillows
- **Marathon kline sarcophagus** (end AD I): figure in heroic semi-nudity; bands for tucking mattress; top sheared off by plow

### Medallion / Clipeus Sarcophagi
Portrait of the deceased displayed in a round **shield-shaped medallion**, flanked by mythological figures.

- **inv. 6701** (Baths of Diocletian, 2nd half AD II): centaurs hold clipeus; Dionysos and maenad ride centaurs; 4 erotes; 2 prisoners under medallion; inscription: "L. Dasumus Germanus, 55 years, paid for by his heirs"
- **Palazzo Torlonia** (near Spanish Steps): reused as horse watering trough; Nereids on hippocamps; spigot = river deity head
- **inv. 708** (ca. AD 160, Luna marble): Cupid and Psyche on ends (Psyche = butterfly = soul; killed by Aphrodite but brought back = immortality); felines play with ribbon at bottom; crown may indicate cult official
- **inv. 125891** (end AD II/beg. AD III): Nikai hold up medallion; crossed cornucopias; sleeping erotes at ends; inscription to Ulpia, wife of Ulpius
- **MC 2772, Monte Martini** (4/4 AD II, Proconnesian marble): winged victories hold uninscribed clypeus; Dionysiac scenes; putti with cock fight; browsing rabbits in corners
- **Menorah sarcophagus** (Baths of Diocletian): medallion has menorah and shofar; putti stomping grapes; two women with herbs; putto with fruit basket and angry goose

### Strigil Sarcophagi
Decorated with repeating **S-shaped strigil curves** (like the scraper used at baths). Cheaper and faster to carve than figural relief.

- **Sarcophagus of Crepereia Tryphaena** (Monte Martini AC 459, ca. AD 150–160): identified by inscription on (now lost) lid
- **MC 1329** (½ AD III): central panel = good shepherd/kriophoros flanked by trees, sheep, dog; left edge = woman with papyrus + Muse; right edge = man with papyrus + Muse
- **Busts sarcophagus** (Baths of Diocletian): wife left in peplos/chiton; husband right with military-style garment clasp; plinths left blank
- **Helios/Selene sarcophagus** (Baths of Diocletian): medallions of Sun (left) and Moon (right) instead of portraits of deceased
- **Church of St Mary of Angels** (Baths of Diocletian, converted): strigil sarcophagus reused as cistern for baptismal font; spigot of re-used material; rests on ashlar building blocks; centre left blank
- **Naples, Gates of Hades** (½ AD III): doors to Hades in centre → repurposed by early Christians as Gates of Heaven; portrait busts on corners with hollow necks for inserted portrait heads; curule chair on end = top vote-getter in civic office; early Christian cross in centre

### Figural Relief Sarcophagi
- **inv. 326137** (¼ AD III, Baths of Diocletian): drinking contest of Herakles and Dionysos; Dionysiac worship tied to **Orphism** = promise of afterlife (Titans devour Dionysos → humans created from Titan ashes = horrid but with divine spark)
- **Naples, Sarcophagus of a Prostitute**: woman with bangles and exposed breast; erotes harvesting grapes; bull scene; camp-style eating erotes
- **Naples, Drinking Contest of Dionysos and Herakles** (early AD III): Dionysos on chariot pulled by centaur; Eros on centaur with lyre; two cista with snakes (possible Isis-Osiris/Orphic/Mithraic symbol); amphora on its side for wine
- **inv. 18** (Baths of Diocletian): lid and body don't match (ca. AD 270 lid; later body); pastoral scenes; dedicated to Julius Achilleus, head of archives → procurator of Ludus Magnus (gladiatorial training ground)
- **MC 839** (Monte Martini, end AD III): clipeus portrait held by winged erotes; figure holds papyrus roll = education; theatre masks; seasons in corners; uncarved back = placed in niche

### Columnar / Panel Sarcophagi
- **Naples inv. 6756** (AD II): "metopes" of the Muses between pilasters; Calliope, Apollo, Zeus, Muses, Melpomene, Thalia; inscription identifies Statilia, daughter of a Decurion (former mayor)
- **Terme Museum** (ca. AD 280–290): Muses in shell-topped niches; theatre masks; short sides each show two Muses flanking a philosopher; found near St. Paul Outside the Walls
- **Galleria Borghese** (ca. AD 160): Labours of Herakles in columnar frame
- **Antakya Musesi** (ca. AD 160): column mosaic of Labours of Herakles; looted WWI → found Geneva → repatriated 2015

## PART 4: EARLY CHRISTIAN BURIAL

### Key Shift: From Cremation to Inhumation
- Christianity required **inhumation** (burial of whole body) due to belief in bodily resurrection — reuniting body and soul
- End of widespread cremation practice

### Imperial Christian Monuments
- **Mausoleum of Constantia / Sta Costanza** (ca. AD 338–354): tomb of daughter of Constantine I; shape of a martyrion/baptistery (both octagonal); built over tomb of St. Agnes (martyred AD 304); plan echoes central Pantheon; ambulatory mosaics appropriate **Dionysiac imagery** (satyrs stomping grapes) for Christian meaning
    - Apse mosaic: Christ on rock with 4 streams of paradise, Sts Peter and Paul
- **Mausoleum of Galla Placidia, Ravenna** (AD 438): actually resting place of Honorius (d. AD 423) and Constantius III (d. AD 421) — Galla Placidia herself is NOT buried here; cross-and-square plan; mosaic swags; starry ceiling; dedicated to St. Lawrence; lunette = book case with 4 Gospels; apse = martyrdom of St. Lawrence on coals
- **Mausoleum of Ste. Helen, Istanbul** (AD 326–330): begun as mausoleum for Constantine, assigned to his mother when she died AD 330; circular form = imperial tradition + martyrdom symbolism

### Catacombs
Dug in **tufa** (soft rock); largely unique to the Rome area; more Jewish catacombs than Christian.

- **Catacomb of St Sebastian** (one of the earliest, early AD II): on the Appian Way; 4 stories underground; always been known and visited; loculi for bodies; wall fresco in Trajanic style; scenes: Apocalypse of St John, death of St John (Ephesus), death of Virgin Mary (Ephesus)
- **Catacomb of St Callistus**: gallery of early popes (AD 199–203, all martyred); loculi of devout; remained popular after legalization of Christianity because of martyred popes' burials
- **Coemeterium Maius** (late AD III): woman officiating at service with hands raised (orans pose); earthy paradise scene; two graves cut into fresco later — one adult, one child
- **Catacomb della Via Latina, Cubiculum M**: appropriation of pagan symbolism — Alcestis (rescued from underworld by Herakles), Orpheus (immortal prophet), Eros-Psyche, cupids as victories, peacocks as immortality symbols
- **Catacomb of St Priscilla** (AD II–IV): originally pagan solution to space shortage along main roads

### Early Christian Funerary Inscriptions & Objects
- **Oldest Christian inscription in Rome** (Baths of Diocletian, early AD 200s): Greek inscription; "Here, Protos lies with the Holy Ghost; Firmima, his sister, in memory out of respect"
- **Funerary stele of Licina Amias** (Baths of Diocletian, AD III, from Vatican necropolis): DM = to the gods; _Ichtheus zonton_ = "fish of those living [after death]"; anchor = salvation; mixture of Christian and pagan symbolism
- **Stele of Priscus** (late AD IV): 36 years old; died 28 May; set up by brother; _in pace_ = "rest in peace" = resides with Christ; orans pose; dove with olive branch; Christogram
- **Lead ash urn** (Baths of Diocletian, late Roman): three nested urns (like Russian dolls); outer = plain; middle = magical formula _Ablatanabla_; inner = Abraxas + INDDD (Jesus of Nazarus, God God God); wax figure of male placed head-down inside — highly unusual as Christians rarely cremated; represents early Christian mysticism connecting lead with averting the evil eye
- **Infant burial** (Phraidaki, Crete): buried in a cista (box normally for cremation) under doorstep — cista used only for children in Christian context
- **Tile graves** (Patras, AD V–VI): simple and cheap — early Christianity expected an imminent Second Coming, so no reason for expensive tombs

### Old and New Testament Sarcophagi
- **MC 2400** (¼ AD IV, Via Banchi Nuovi): Raising of Lazarus; rooster = denial of Peter; Moses receiving Law; vessels at feet of Christ = Marriage at Cana; Moses striking rock for water
- **Concordia University sarcophagus** (late AD IV): battle scene + OT/NT figures — Sacrifice of Isaac, Healing of Blind Man, Loaves and Fishes, Denial of Peter, etc.
- **Leiden, Passion of Christ** (late AD IV): columnar sarcophagus; scenes from last days of Christ
- **Sarcophagus of Helen, mother of Constantine** (ca. AD 325, porphyry): battle scenes; Via Labicana; Helen founded St Peter's in Rome, church in Jerusalem, St Catherine's Monastery; credited with discovering the True Cross; her example made pilgrimages popular

### Lead Sarcophagus (Musée des Beaux Arts, Montréal)
- Ca. late AD II – AD IV; probably from Tyre (Lebanon)
- Made using clay moulds on which melted lead was poured; bottom and sides joined by lead
- Body placed inside and covered in **honey** as preservative
- Stamped designs: columns, Medusa masks on swags, sphinxes, kantharoi, dolphins, plants

## PART 5: RESURRECTION MYTHS

### Key Theme
Death in the ancient world was not always permanent — many myths deal with resurrection, rejuvenation, and immortality.

### Myths and Figures
**Iolaos (son of Hercules)**
- Rejuvenated for _one day_ to defeat enemy Eurystheus in single combat (Euripides, _Heracleidae_)
- Shows immortality need not be permanent; nymphs could be given 99 years of youth

**Dying and Rising Gods of Vegetation**
- Many eastern Mediterranean deities died in Fall and were resurrected in Spring
- Mt. Juktas (Crete): topography resembles face of Zeus — Cretans claimed to show his tomb → gave them reputation as liars
- Persephone: unusual in merely _going away_ for 3 months (not dying)

**Dionysos**
- Titans devour Dionysos; Zeus destroys Titans; humans created from Titan ashes → humans are horrid (Titan inheritance) but have divine spark (Dionysiac inheritance)
- Dionysos born a second time (by Semele via his heart)
- Devotees (initially male only) could petition for eternal life

**Herakles**
- After death and cremation on Mt. Oeta, deified and joined the gods of Olympus
- Made immortal by nursing at Hera's breast (Etruscan mirror, Volterra, ca. 325–300 BC) — depicted as adult
- Married Hebe (daughter of Zeus and Hera); became god of fevers and headaches
- Apotheosis depicted on black-figure amphora (ca. 530–520 BC): Athena drives chariot, Herakles walks beside horses

**Glaukos and Polyidus**
- Glaukos (son of Minos) drowns in a vat of honey
- Polyidus imprisoned; witnesses a snake revive its dead mate using an herb
- Uses the herb to revive Glaukos → Glaukos immortal
- BUT: Polyidus asks Glaukos to spit in his mouth before leaving → Glaukos forgets everything
- Depicted on white-ground cup by Sotades (British Museum)

**Orpheus and Orphism**
- Maenads kill Orpheus and sever his head
- Severed head gives oracles → beginning of Orphism
- Gold tablets (_lamella_) associated with Orphic cult

**Psyche (from Apuleius, _Golden Ass_ / _Metamorphoses_ 5.7–10)**
- Cupid ashamed/afraid; Psyche doesn't listen; sisters greedy and envious; Venus = evil mother-in-law
- Love conquers all: Psyche given nectar and ambrosia → becomes a deity (immortal)
- Depicted in Sousse (Tunisia) Museum of Mosaics: Athena helps Psyche in a challenge

### Immortality Objects
- **Cage Cup, Novara** (AD IV): inscription reads _BIBE VIVAS MULTIS ANNIS_ = "Drink so that you may live many years"
- **Pomegranate**: sign of achieving the afterlife (appears on kline sarcophagi)
- **Eggs**: symbol of having achieved afterlife (found in cremation kits)
- **Swan**: widely accepted sign of immortality (appears on cinerary urns)
- **Palmette** (upside down): widely accepted sign of immortality (feet of house-style urns)
- **Peacock**: animal of immortality (catacomb imagery)
- **Ivy leaf**: starts as pagan Dionysiac imagery; early adopted by Christians as sign of afterlife
