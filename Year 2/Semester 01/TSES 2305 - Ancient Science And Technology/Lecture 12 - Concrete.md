### **Water and Waste Management**

Ancient urban planning prioritized the movement of waste and water, often utilizing open systems and specialized materials. The **Panathenaic Way**, the most significant street in ancient Athens, featured an open sewer system (Image 3) where water and waste would occasionally seep into the street at the joins between sections. To mitigate this, settling basins were placed at several points along the route. During the **Roman Empire**, more sophisticated ceramic drains were implemented west of the **Theatre of Dionysos** (Image 4). These consisted of U-shaped lower sections with curved cover tiles joined by cement, sometimes running directly beneath earlier Greek structures. In **Herculaneum** (Images 5–6), sewers ran alongside the roads. Because waste was frequently tossed directly from windows, the berms on the sides of these sewers often show undercutting from constant flushing. At the **Baths of Galerius in Thessaloniki** (**ca. AD 300**), construction utilized a specific brick-to-mortar ratio and thick stucco to ensure water-tightness (Images 7–8).

### **Hydraulic Engineering and Cisterns**

The management of fresh water required advanced hydraulic cement, often mixed with crushed pot sherds to improve durability. In **Aptera, Crete**, dating to the **second century AD (AD ii)**, triple-chamber barrel vault cisterns were constructed uphill from bath complexes to provide high water pressure (Images 9–14). These structures were designed with raised lower edges to deter the growth of algae. Similarly, at a Roman villa in **San Vicenzino** (also **AD ii**), an ingenious system collected rainwater from the _impluvium_ (reflecting pool) and channeled it through a hole into a cistern below (Image 21). In later periods, such as the **Medieval era** at **Hosios Loukas** in Greece, builders utilized groin vaults and specialized water wells (Images 15–17) to continue this tradition of water storage. By the **sixth century AD (post AD vi)**, however, construction sometimes became more "slapdash," as seen in the Byzantine wells of the Athens Agora (Images 19–20).

### **Domestic and Social Architecture**

Roman housing reflected a clear social and functional hierarchy. The **Roman Atrium House** was divided into public front areas and private back areas (Images 33–35). The atrium served as a central space for the women of the household, with windows looking inward, while the peristyle (garden) and dining rooms were considered more masculine spaces. For the working classes, multi-story tenements known as **_insulae_** were common. At the **Caseggiato di Diana in Ostia**, the ground floor was used for shops, while upper floors were residential (Images 66–69). Social status was tied to elevation: the first floor held expensive apartments, while the third floor contained cheap garrets. These buildings often featured communal areas for cooking and cleaning (Images 71–72) and occasionally housed religious shrines, such as a **Mithraeum**, in the basement or nearby alleys (Images 73–74).

### **Public Monuments and Infrastructure**

Major public works demonstrated the scale of Roman ambition and the use of forced labor. The **Colosseum** (Images 47–51), built by 100,000 Jewish captives following the looting of the Temple of Solomon, was a masterpiece of weight distribution, using calibrated arches to channel force. In **Pompeii**, the **Temple of Jupiter** (Image 41) sat on the central square, featuring a brick core with travertine facing. The city's infrastructure was tested by a major earthquake in **AD 62** before being buried by Mount Vesuvius in **AD 79**. Public health was also central to the city, as seen in the **Baths of Caracalla**, which featured intricate geometric and athletic mosaics (Images 82–84). These baths were so structurally sound that they were repurposed for the gymnastics events of the **1960 Rome Olympics**.

### **Structural Mechanics and Earthquake Resilience**

Roman engineering was defined by the use of **brick-faced concrete** to manage weight and prevent fires. Arches were used to radiate force outward at 45-degree angles (Images 24–27). Builders also utilized "hidden arches" (Image 115) to divert weight and prevent walls from collapsing under stress. In areas prone to seismic activity, such as Athens, engineers developed "earthquake-proofing" techniques. Step treads were sometimes set in sections (Image 110) so they would shift rather than break during a tremor, allowing them to be easily reset or replaced. Walls were often double-faced with blocks set crosswise (Images 111–112), enabling a "snake-like" motion during an earthquake that prevented total collapse. Additionally, cross-walls might include spaces for planters (Images 113–114) to relieve the weight of the structure while providing cooling and aesthetic benefits. For large roofing projects, engineers hypothetically used **truss roofs** (Image 116), where beams were tied with bronze jackets to create a cantilever effect, essentially mimicking a dome in wood.
