# DRY SET CONSTRUCTION — NOTES

## 1. What is Dry Set Construction?

- Building technique using **stone without mortar or cement**
- Stability comes from:
    - **Weight (gravity)**
    - **Precise fitting of stones**
- Still used when:
    - Faster
    - Cheaper
    - Easier to repair

---

## 2. Early Examples (Neolithic)

### Skara Brae (ca. 3000 BC)

- Semi-underground stone houses
- Features:
    - Central hearth
    - Shelves + room divisions
- Evidence of:
    - Large gatherings (400+ cattle bones)
    - Trade networks

### Ness of Brodgar

- Early houses:
    - Thick double walls (insulation + support)
    - Truss roof systems
- Structural innovation:
    - Walls support roof pressure

---

## 3. Basic Principles of Construction

- Limited ways to build due to:
    - Human body constraints
    - Materials available
- Architecture follows **consistent “taxonomy” (patterns)**

---

## 4. Key Structural Concepts

### Post and Lintel System

- Two vertical supports + horizontal beam
- Problems:
    - Compression at joints
    - Lintel sagging (tension)

### Stone Limitations

- Weak in **tension**
- Strong in **compression**
- Result:
    - Columns must be **close together**
    - Lintels must be **short**

---

## 5. Dry Set Techniques

### Corbelled Arch

- Stones overlap inward gradually
- No true arch shape
- Forces balance:
    - Inward push
    - Outward push
    - Gravity

### Relieving Triangle

- Gap above a lintel
- Reduces weight pressure
- Common in Mycenaean structures

---

- Storage rooms ("magazines")

## 7. Engineering for Earthquakes

### Clamps (horizontal stability)

- Metal pieces joining stones
- Set in **lead**
- During earthquakes:
    - Lead melts → allows movement
    - Re-hardens after

### Dowels (vertical stability)

- Connect stacked stones
- Also set in lead

### Precision Fitting

- Stones fit within **1–3 mm**
- Reduces shaking damage

---

### Lifting Techniques

- **Lewis clamp**

### Pry Holes

- Used to adjust stone positioning

## 9. Roads, Walls, and Infrastructure

- Roads = always dry set
- Advantages:
    - Easy repair
- Retaining walls:
    - Long stones at base
    - Smaller stones above

---

## 10. Greek Temple Engineering

### Optical Refinements (Parthenon)

- Columns bulge slightly (entasis)
- Columns lean inward
- Stylobate curves upward
- Purpose:
    - Make building look perfectly straight

---

## 11. Development of the Arch

### Early (Corbelled)

- Weak and unstable
- Often buried in earth for support

### True Arch (Roman)

- Uses **voussoirs (wedge stones)**
- Keystone locks structure
- Forces distributed outward

### Key Parts:

- Voussoirs
- Keystone
- Spandrel
- Impost

---

## 12. Flat Arch

- Uses angled bricks
- Needs:
    - Side pressure
    - Concrete/mortar
- Often includes relieving triangle

---

## 13. Roman Innovations

### Concrete (Hydraulic Cement)

- Strong + water-resistant
- Allows:
    - Larger structures
    - Vaults and domes

---

### Wall Types

#### Opus Incertum (earliest)

- Random stones in concrete

#### Opus Reticulatum

- Diamond pattern (net-like)

#### Opus Testaceum

- Brick facing
- Most advanced

---

## 14. Vaults

### Barrel Vault

- Tunnel shape

### Groin Vault

- Two barrel vaults intersect
- Advantages:
    - Less material
    - More openings
    - Better for large spaces

---

## 15. Key Materials

### Stone

- Strong in compression
- Weak in tension

### Wood

- Strong but rots

### Brick

- Lighter and cheaper
- Needs mortar

### Concrete

- Strong in all directions
- Major Roman advancement

---

## 16. Important Themes

### Architecture Depends On:

- Available materials
- Technology (tools)
- Environment (earthquakes, climate)

---

### Dry Set Advantages:

- Flexible in earthquakes
- Easy to repair
- No need for mortar

### Dry Set Disadvantages:

- Limited height/complexity
- Requires precision cutting
