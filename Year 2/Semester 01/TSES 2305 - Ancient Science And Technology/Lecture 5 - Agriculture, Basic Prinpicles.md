
# Agriculture Is Historical, Not “Natural”

#### Core Principle
> **All crops are modified crops**

- Humans have influenced plant evolution since at least the **Mesolithic**
- Seed-saving = **artificial selection**
    - Largest, sweetest, hardiest plants chosen
    - Replanted year after year
- Result:
    - Modern “natural” foods are heavily engineered (organically, not genetically)



---
# Old World vs. New World Foods

#### Key Idea
> **More than half of modern European cuisine relies on New World crops**

- Modern Greek agriculture dominated by:
    - Potatoes
    - Tomatoes
    - Corn
- These **did not exist in antiquity**

#### Exam Implication
- Ancient recipes (e.g. **Apicius**) must be understood with:
    - Fewer ingredients
    - Different flavours
    - Limited variety



---
# Missing Old World Crops

#### Potatoes
- Origin: **South America**
- Entirely absent from antiquity
- Now a major staple in Europe

#### Tomatoes
- Origin: **North America**
- Initially thought poisonous in Europe
    - Associated with witchcraft
    - Some deaths reinforced fear

#### Corn (Maize)
- Origin: **Southern Mexico**
- Early corn:
    - Very small kernels
    - Limited usefulness
- Gradual improvement through seed selection
    - Example of **long-term organic engineering**



---
# Scale and Organization of Modern Agriculture

#### Modern Food Processing
- Crops today undergo:
    - Harvest
    - Transport
    - Washing
    - Cutting
    - Packaging
- Even in traditionally “local” places (e.g., Crete)

#### Example
- Potatoes:
    - Grown → shipped → washed → sliced → sold to restaurants
- USDA (2023):
    - Americans eat frozen meals **2.7 days/week**

#### Exam Takeaway
> **Ancient agriculture lacked this scale, specialization, and infrastructure**



---
# Other New World or Post-Antique Crops

#### Bananas
- Ancient Mediterranean bananas:
    - Smaller
    - Sweeter
- Modern commercial bananas:
    - Larger
    - Less sweet
- Shows **selective breeding priorities differ**

#### Carob
- Now used as chocolate substitute
- Introduced to Europe after **Columbus’ second voyage**
- Cannot function as “chocolate substitute” before chocolate exists

#### Prickly Pear (Opuntia)
- Introduced by **Spanish conquistadores**
- Nearly all cactus in Europe = prickly pear
- Common in:
    - Preserves
    - Spoon sweets

# What the Ancients Knew but Did Not Exploit

#### Butter
- Discovered accidentally (like yogurt)
- Early evidence:
    - Caucasus Mountains, c. **9000 BC**
    - Near East, c. **2500 BC**
- Mediterranean antiquity:
    - Very little evidence of butter use
- Possible reason:
    - Cultural association with **nomadic pastoralism**
- Modern butter:
    - ~81% fat
    - Salted for preservation

#### Exam Insight
> Knowledge does not guarantee adoption — culture matters



---
# Sugar (Post-Antique Development)

#### Sugar Beet
- Developed in **Germany**
- A.S. Markgraf (1747):
    - Extracted sugar from beets
- More expensive than cane sugar
- Spread to North America via German immigrants

#### Canada
- Sugar beets important cash crop until WWII
- Japanese Canadians interned during WWII:
    - Offered labor on sugar beet farms

#### Exam Relevance
- Demonstrates:
    - Agricultural technology = political + social history
    - Crops reshape labor systems



---
# Big Comparative Idea (Harrison’s Style)

> **What matters is not what existed, but what was used, scaled, and culturally accepted**

- Ancient people:
    - Knew many plants
    - Had access to land and labor
- But lacked:
    - Global exchange
    - Industrial processing
    - Crop standardization



---
# Summary

- “All crops are modified through seed selection.”
- “More than half of modern European cuisine depends on New World crops.”
- “Ancient recipes must be read with absent ingredients in mind.”
- “Knowledge of a food does not guarantee its cultural adoption.”
- “Modern agriculture reorganizes landscapes and labor in ways impossible in antiquity.”
